Detailed Quick Recall Notes: Contact Lens Steps 26-38

Step 26: Lens Care & Hygiene Instructions

Hand Washing Rub & Rinse Disinfection Lens Case Care MPS Hydrogen Peroxide Tap Water Warning Compliance
Patient Safety Cornerstone: Improper care is a leading cause of serious CL complications.

Core Principles for All Lens Types:

  1. HAND WASHING: Thoroughly with soap & water, dry with lint-free towel BEFORE every lens handling.
  2. Follow ECP & Manufacturer instructions.
  3. NEVER Use Tap Water (or saliva, homemade solutions) on lenses or cases – risk of Acanthamoeba!
  4. Lens Case Care: Rinse with fresh disinfecting solution (not water), air dry upside down with caps off. Replace case regularly (e.g., every 1-3 months).
  5. No "Topping Off" Solution: Always discard old solution, use fresh.
  6. Adhere to prescribed lens replacement schedule.

Basic Regimen Steps (General - varies by solution):

StepSoft Lenses (Most MPS)RGP Lenses
1. CLEANPlace in palm, add MPS, RUB gently ~20s/side. ("Rub & Rinse" is vital).Place in palm, add RGP cleaner, RUB gently ~20s/side.
2. RINSEThoroughly rinse with MPS or sterile saline.Thoroughly rinse with saline or RGP-approved MPS/rinse.
3. DISINFECT/STOREPlace in clean case with fresh MPS. Soak per instructions (e.g., 4-6h).Place in clean case with fresh RGP conditioning/disinfecting solution. Soak.

Hydrogen Peroxide (H2O2) Systems: Require special neutralizing case (e.g., catalytic disc). NEVER put unneutralized H2O2 in eye! Preservative-free once neutralized.

Hygiene Mantra: "Hands, Rub, Rinse, Fresh Solution, Clean Case, Right Schedule!"
Takeaway: Meticulous hand hygiene and adherence to the correct "rub, rinse, disinfect, store" steps with fresh solutions and a clean case are non-negotiable for preventing CL-related infections.
Self-Check: Why is rubbing the lens during cleaning still recommended even if a solution is labelled "no-rub"?

Rubbing manually dislodges significantly more deposits (proteins, lipids, debris) and reduces microbial bioburden from the lens surface than rinsing alone, even with "no-rub" solutions. Most ECPs strongly advocate for the "rub and rinse" step to enhance safety and lens performance.

Step 27: Patient Compliance

Adherence Non-Compliance Reasons Consequences (MK, GPC) Improving Compliance Education Communication

Understanding Compliance:

  • Compliance: The extent to which a patient follows prescribed lens wear schedule, hygiene practices, solution use, and follow-up appointments.
  • Non-Compliance is VERY Common.

Common Reasons for Non-Compliance:

  • Forgetting instructions / Misunderstanding complex regimens.
  • Perceived inconvenience, "too busy," laziness.
  • Cost of solutions or frequent replacement lenses.
  • Belief that "shortcuts" (e.g., topping off, tap water rinse, overwear) are harmless.
  • Lack of immediate negative feedback (until a problem arises).
  • Comfort with lenses leading to complacency over time.
  • Peer influence or misinformation.

Major Consequences of Non-Compliance:

  • Microbial Keratitis (MK): Risk increases dramatically.
  • Other infections and inflammatory events (CLARE, infiltrates, CLPU).
  • Giant Papillary Conjunctivitis (GPC) due to deposit buildup from poor cleaning/overwear.
  • Hypoxic complications from overwear.
  • Discomfort, reduced wear time, lens intolerance, financial cost of treating complications.

Strategies to Improve Patient Compliance:

  1. Clear, Simple, Repeated Education: Verbal, written (brochures, personalized notes), and demonstrated instructions. Use "teach-back."
  2. Emphasize Risks & Benefits: Explain *why* each step is vital for eye health, not just a rule.
  3. Simplify Regimen: If complex care is a barrier, consider daily disposables.
  4. Personalize Instructions: Tailor to patient's lifestyle and understanding.
  5. Regular Follow-ups: Opportunity to review, reinforce, and detect non-compliance early.
  6. Build Rapport & Trust: Non-judgmental questioning (e.g., "Tell me about your usual evening routine with your lenses...").
  7. Utilize Reminders: Lens replacement schedules, solution purchase.
Analogy - Compliance like Following a Prescription for Medicine: "Your lens care routine is like a doctor's prescription. If you skip doses (don't clean), use the wrong 'medicine' (tap water), or take it for too long (overwear lenses), you won't get the benefits and might get serious side effects (eye infections). We need to find a 'prescription' (lens type and care) that works for your 'lifestyle' and that you can follow consistently."
Takeaway: Patient compliance is a cornerstone of safe CL wear. ECPs play a crucial role in education, motivation, and simplifying regimens to minimize risks associated with non-adherence.
Self-Check: A patient consistently overwears their monthly lenses to "save money." What is a key risk you would emphasize, and what alternative might you discuss?

A key risk to emphasize is the significantly increased chance of Microbial Keratitis and other inflammatory complications due to excessive deposit buildup and material degradation. An alternative to discuss might be switching to a more cost-effective brand of monthly lenses if available, exploring vision plans/rebates, or, if feasible for their budget over time, explaining that daily disposables (while seemingly more expensive upfront for full-time wear) eliminate solution costs and many compliance-related risks, potentially saving money on treating complications in the long run. Discussing less frequent wear (e.g., part-time) if full-time is a financial stretch for monthly lenses might also be an option if visually appropriate.

Step 28: Follow-up Post Fitting Examination (General Protocol)

Follow-up Schedule History Taking Visual Acuity (VA) Over-Refraction (ORx) Slit Lamp Exam (Lens ON & OFF) Corneal Staining Patient Management

Purpose & Typical Schedule:

  • Purpose: Monitor ocular health, ensure optimal lens performance (vision, comfort, fit), reinforce compliance, detect early signs of complications.
  • Typical Schedule (can vary):
    • Initial (New Fit): ~1-2 weeks after dispensing.
    • Subsequent: ~1 month, ~3 months, then ~6 months.
    • Established Wearer: Annually (or every 6 months if higher risk/EW).

Key Components of a Follow-up Visit:

  1. History Taking (Subjective Assessment):
    • Average Wearing Time (AWT) per day? Days per week? Any overnight wear?
    • Comfort: Overall, initial, end-of-day? Dryness, itching, FBS?
    • Vision: Clear, stable, fluctuating? Glare, halos? Distance, intermediate, near performance?
    • Lens Care Habits: Review compliance (solutions, rubbing, case care, replacement schedule). Ask open-ended questions.
    • Any problems: RSVP - Redness, Sensitivity, Vision change, Pain? Discharge?
  2. Objective Assessment:
    • Visual Acuity (VA): With current CLs (monocular & binocular). Compare to baseline/target.
    • Over-Refraction (ORx): To check if lens power is still optimal or if refractive error has changed.
    • Slit Lamp Examination - Lenses ON (Step 29 details this):
      • Assess lens fit (centration, movement, coverage).
      • Check lens surface (deposits, wettability, defects).
    • Patient Removes Lenses (observe their technique if concerned about handling).
    • Slit Lamp Examination - Lenses OFF:
      • Corneal Integrity: Fluorescein staining is CRITICAL. Look for SPK, abrasions, infiltrates, ulcers, edema (striae, microcysts), neovascularization, warpage.
      • Conjunctiva: Hyperemia, papillae (evert upper lid for GPC), follicles, chemosis.
      • Lids/Lashes: Blepharitis, MGD, lid margin health.
      • Tear Film: Re-assess TBUT, meniscus, quality.
  3. Discussion & Plan:
    • Address any issues identified (subjective or objective).
    • Reinforce proper care, hygiene, and compliance.
    • Make necessary changes: Lens parameters, material, modality, solutions.
    • Schedule next follow-up appointment appropriately.
    • Document all findings and recommendations thoroughly.
Analogy - CL Follow-up like a Car Service: "Your eyes wearing CLs are like a high-performance car. Follow-ups are regular 'service appointments.'
  1. Driver Interview (History): 'How's the car running? Any strange noises (discomfort)? Vision clear through the windshield?'
  2. Performance Check (VA, ORx): 'Is it still getting good mileage (VA)? Does the engine need a tune-up (ORx)?'
  3. Exterior Check (Lens ON): 'Are the tires (lenses) fitting well, clean, no damage?'
  4. Under the Hood (Lens OFF): 'Check the engine parts (cornea) for wear and tear (staining, inflammation). Change the oil (reinforce care).'
  5. Service Report & Next Service (Plan): 'Everything's good / We need to adjust X / Come back in Y months.'"
Takeaway: Regular, comprehensive follow-up examinations are non-negotiable for maintaining long-term ocular health and success in contact lens wearers. They allow for early problem detection and intervention.
Self-Check: During a follow-up with lenses off, you observe superior arcuate staining with fluorescein. What CL-related issue might this indicate, and what would you check with lenses on?

Superior arcuate staining often indicates a Superior Epithelial Arcuate Lesion (SEAL). This is typically a mechanical issue caused by a stiffer or thicker superior edge of a soft contact lens (often a higher modulus Silicone Hydrogel) chafing against the superior cornea/limbus.
With lenses ON, you would carefully assess the lens fit, particularly looking for a tight fit superiorly, insufficient movement, or a prominent/poorly designed superior lens edge that could be interacting with the upper lid and superior cornea during blinks.

Step 29: Follow-up Slit Lamp Examination with Contact Lenses (Lens ON eye)

Lens Fit Re-assessment Lens Surface (Deposits, Wettability, Defects) Corneal Edema Signs Limbal Hyperemia Pre-Lens Tear Film

Key Assessment Areas (Lens ON):

  1. Lens Fit (Dynamic & Static Re-assessment):
    • Soft Lenses: Confirm good Centration (pupil covered), Coverage (cornea fully covered + scleral overlap), and Movement (0.2-0.5mm on push-up/blink).
      • Look for edge fluting (loose) or conjunctival indentation/vessel blanching at limbus (tight).
    • RGP Lenses: Re-assess fluorescein pattern (apical, mid-peripheral, peripheral clearance/touch) and lens movement/positioning. Check for decentration, excessive bearing, inadequate edge lift, or bubbles under lens (can indicate poor wetting or edge issues).
    • Toric Lenses: Check rotational stability and orientation markings (Step 32).
    • Multifocal Lenses: Assess centration over pupil, movement (especially for translating RGPs).
  2. Lens Surface Condition & Integrity:
    • Deposits: Use diffuse and focal illumination, various magnifications.
      • Proteins (whitish films, hazy appearance).
      • Lipids (greasy, smudgy globules).
      • Mucin/Calcium "Jelly Bumps" (raised, discrete bumps).
      • Makeup, environmental debris.
      Relevance: Deposits reduce comfort, VA, Dk/t; increase GPC risk, microbial adhesion.
    • Wettability: Observe pre-lens tear film. Does it spread evenly or break up quickly into dry spots (hydrophobic areas)? Use broad tangential beam or specular reflection. Relevance: Poor wettability → dryness, discomfort, fluctuating VA.
    • Lens Defects/Damage: Scratches, chips (RGPs), tears/nicks (soft lenses), crazing (fine cracks in older RGPs). Relevance: Compromise comfort, vision, structural integrity; can harbor microbes. Defective lenses must be replaced.
  3. Corneal & Conjunctival Signs (Visible with Lens On):
    • Corneal Edema: Vertical striae (posterior stroma), epithelial microcysts (chronic hypoxia), diffuse stromal haze (Sattler's veil if acute & severe). Relevance: Indicates hypoxic stress or tight fit.
    • Limbal Hyperemia (Redness): Note degree (mild, mod, severe) and location (sectoral, circumlimbal). Relevance: Can indicate tight fit, hypoxia, solution reaction, inflammation, dry eye.
    • Trapped Debris/Bubbles: Under lens, especially RGPs. May indicate poor fit, poor tear exchange, or handling issues.
  4. Pre-Lens Tear Film: Assess quality, quantity, and stability (observe for quick break-up over the lens surface).
Analogy - Checking a Car with New Tires On: "The lens-on exam is like checking a car *after* new tires (CLs) have been put on and driven a bit.
  • Lens Fit: 'Are the tires centered on the wheels? Do they wobble (move too much) or are they stuck (too tight)?'
  • Lens Surface: 'Are the tires clean (no deposits)? Is the rubber smooth or scuffed (wettability, defects)?'
  • Car's Reaction (Cornea): 'Is the car frame (cornea) showing stress from the tires (edema, redness at edges)?'"
Takeaway: The slit lamp exam with lenses ON provides crucial information about how the lens is performing dynamically on the eye, its surface condition, and any immediate physiological responses it's inducing. It's a key part of every follow-up.
Self-Check: You observe a soft contact lens on a patient's eye that shows no movement with the push-up test, and there's visible blanching of the conjunctival vessels at the lens edge. What does this indicate, and what is a potential risk?

This indicates a very tight (steep) soft lens fit. The lack of movement and vessel blanching (constriction of blood vessels due to pressure) are classic signs. A potential risk is significant corneal hypoxia due to restricted tear exchange and oxygen flow, leading to corneal edema, neovascularization, and increased risk of inflammatory or infectious complications like CLARE or even MK if debris gets trapped.
