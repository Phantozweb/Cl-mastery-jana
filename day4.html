Step 13: Placido's Disc

A simple, handheld instrument used for qualitative assessment of the anterior corneal surface regularity.

  • Principle: Consists of a series of concentric black and white rings. The reflection of these rings (mires) off the patient's cornea is observed through a central aperture.
  • Interpretation:
    • Smooth, regular, circular, and evenly spaced rings: Indicate a normal, regular corneal surface.
    • Distorted, irregular, or bunched-up rings: Indicate an irregular corneal surface (e.g., keratoconus, corneal warpage from CLs, dry spots breaking up the reflection, scars).
      • Rings closer together = steeper curvature in that area.
      • Rings further apart = flatter curvature.
      • Oval rings = astigmatism.
  • Advantages: Simple, inexpensive, quick. Good for initial screening.
  • Limitations: Qualitative (not precise measurements), highly dependent on tear film stability, only assesses the area of cornea reflecting the rings.
Analogy - Placido's Disc: Like looking at the reflection of a perfectly striped beach ball on a calm pool of water (regular cornea) versus a wavy pool (irregular cornea). The reflection gets distorted by the waves.

Step 14: Corneal Topography (Corneal Mapping)

A sophisticated, computerized instrument that provides a quantitative and qualitative map of the entire corneal surface curvature and shape.

Common Technologies:

  • Placido-based Systems: Project concentric rings onto the cornea and analyze their reflected image. Most common type. Measures anterior surface.
  • Scheimpflug Imaging Systems: Use a rotating camera to take cross-sectional images of the anterior segment. Can measure anterior and posterior corneal surfaces, corneal thickness (pachymetry), and anterior chamber depth. (e.g., Pentacam, Orbscan).

Types of Maps & Interpretation:

Color Scales: Standardized color codes represent curvature. Typically: Warm colors (reds, oranges) = Steeper areas. Cool colors (blues, greens) = Flatter areas.

Map Type What it Shows Clinical Use
Axial (Sagittal) Map Curvature relative to the optical axis. Smoother, good for overall shape. Power map. General overview, refractive surgery planning, identifying astigmatism type (WTR, ATR, Oblique), basic keratoconus screening.
Tangential (Instantaneous) Map Curvature at specific points, more sensitive to local changes. "True" curvature map. More precise localization of corneal apex in keratoconus, identifying peripheral corneal changes, post-refractive surgery ectasia.
Elevation Map Height of the cornea relative to a reference sphere (e.g., "best fit sphere"). Shows high and low spots. Critical for fitting specialty lenses (RGPs, sclerals) as it shows where the lens will bear or clear. Keratoconus assessment.
Refractive Power Map Corneal power in diopters. Useful for understanding visual impact of corneal shape. Refractive surgery evaluation, understanding visual symptoms.

Key Topographic Indices & Uses:

  • Simulated Keratometry (SimK): K-readings derived from the map.
  • Kmax: Curvature of the steepest point on the cornea. Important for keratoconus.
  • Corneal Eccentricity (e-value): Rate of peripheral corneal flattening. Normal cornea is prolate (flattens peripherally).
  • Detecting Keratoconus: Steep Kmax, inferior steepening, high astigmatism, skewed radial axes, abnormal indices (e.g., I-S value, KISA%).
  • Other Uses: Fitting complex CLs, monitoring corneal disease, pre/post-op surgical assessment, diagnosing corneal warpage from CLs.
Study Tip - Map Interpretation: If you have access to sample topography maps, practice identifying normal patterns, astigmatism types, and signs of keratoconus based on color patterns and indices.

Step 15: Introduction to Contact Lens Fitting (General Principles)

The overall process and philosophy of selecting and evaluating contact lenses for a patient.

Goals of Contact Lens Fitting: "The 3 C's + H"

  1. Clear & Stable Vision: Good visual acuity at all distances, minimal fluctuation.
  2. Comfort: Lens should be comfortable throughout the wearing period.
  3. Corneal Health (and overall ocular health): Lens wear should not compromise the eye's physiology.
  4. (Sometimes added) Handling: Patient should be able to insert, remove, and care for lenses effectively.

Key Steps in the Fitting Process:

  1. Patient History & Needs Assessment:
    • Reason for wanting CLs? Lifestyle? Occupation? Hobbies? Previous CL experience?
    • Medical/Ocular history (allergies, dry eye, systemic conditions - Day 3 contraindications!).
    • Motivation and expectations.
  2. Preliminary Tests / Pre-fitting Examination:
    • Visual Acuity (VA) - unaided and with current correction.
    • Manifest Refraction (accurate spectacle Rx is crucial).
    • Keratometry (K's) - (Day 3).
    • Slit Lamp Examination - detailed assessment of anterior eye health (Day 1).
      • Lids, lashes, conjunctiva, cornea (staining?), tear film (TBUT, quality, quantity).
    • Corneal Topography (if indicated).
    • Other tests: Horizontal Visible Iris Diameter (HVID), pupil size, tear film assessment.
  3. Trial Lens Selection:
    • Based on refraction, K-readings, HVID, and chosen lens type (soft vs. RGP, spherical vs. toric etc.).
    • Initial soft lens BC often chosen based on K's (flatter K's -> flatter BC; steeper K's -> steeper BC, or manufacturer's guidelines).
    • Initial RGP BC often chosen "on K" (aligned with flat K), slightly steeper, or slightly flatter depending on fitting philosophy.
  4. Trial Lens Insertion & Settling Time: Allow lens to settle on the eye (e.g., 10-20 mins for soft, longer for RGP adaptation).
  5. Fit Assessment: (Details in later steps for specific lens types).
  6. Over-Refraction: Refining the lens power with the trial lens on the eye.

Step 16: Handling of Contact Lenses (Insertion & Removal - I&R)

Teaching patients proper handling is CRITICAL for success and safety.

Hygiene, Hygiene, Hygiene! ALWAYS start with thoroughly washed and dried hands.

Practitioner Insertion/Removal (you doing it for the patient initially):

  • Explain each step. Be gentle and confident.
  • Use appropriate solutions (e.g., saline for rinsing).

Patient Insertion & Removal - Soft Lenses:

  1. Preparation:
    • Wash and dry hands thoroughly.
    • Remove lens from case, rinse with multipurpose solution (MPS) or saline.
    • Place lens on dry fingertip (usually index finger).
    • Check for inversion ("Taco Test" - correct way looks like a bowl, inverted looks like a saucer with a lip).
  2. Insertion:
    • Use other hand to pull upper lid up against brow bone.
    • Use middle finger of inserting hand to pull lower lid down.
    • Look straight ahead or slightly up, gently place lens on colored part of eye (iris/cornea).
    • Slowly release lids, blink gently.
  3. Removal:
    • Wash and dry hands.
    • Look up, pull down lower lid.
    • Use index finger to slide lens down onto white part of eye (sclera).
    • Gently pinch lens off between thumb and index finger.

Patient Insertion & Removal - RGP Lenses:

  1. Preparation: Wash hands. Rinse lens with RGP solution or saline. May apply wetting/conditioning solution.
  2. Insertion: Similar to soft, but RGP lens is smaller. May need to ensure it's centered.
  3. Removal Methods:
    • Scissor/Eyelid Method (Common): Look down. Open eyes wide. Use index fingers at outer canthus to pull lids taut towards ear, then "blink" lens out into cupped hand.
    • DMV Suction Cup (Optional): Moisten cup. Gently apply to RGP lens center and pull off. Requires practice.

Common Patient Difficulties & Solutions:

  • Fear of touching eye -> Reassurance, start with touching white part.
  • Lens falling off finger -> Ensure finger is dry, lens correctly oriented.
  • Blinking too much -> Lid control techniques, deep breaths.
  • Lens folding on insertion -> "Taco test," ensure enough solution for lens to float.
  • Difficulty finding lens in eye -> Use mirror, look in different directions.
For I&R Education: "Show, Tell, Do, Review."
1. You SHOW them. 2. You TELL them what you're doing. 3. They DO it with your guidance. 4. You REVIEW their technique.
Study Tip - Practice Explaining: Verbally walk through the I&R steps for both soft and RGP lenses as if you were teaching a new patient. Anticipate their questions.

Handling Scenario: A new soft lens patient keeps blinking the lens out or it folds on their eye before they can place it. What are two key things you would re-emphasize or help them with?

Answer & Rationale

1. Lid Control: Re-emphasize pulling the upper lid firmly up against the brow bone and the lower lid firmly down. Ensure they are creating a wide enough opening and preventing lashes from interfering. Poor lid control is a major reason for blinking the lens out.
2. Lens Orientation & Moisture: Double-check they are doing the "taco test" correctly to ensure the lens isn't inverted (an inverted lens is more likely to fold or not adhere). Ensure there's a small droplet of saline or MPS on the lens so it can "float" onto the cornea rather than sticking to their finger, but not so much that it runs everywhere. A dry finger is also key.
