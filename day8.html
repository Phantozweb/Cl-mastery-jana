Step 29: Follow-up Slit Lamp Examination with Contact Lenses (Lens ON eye)

This is part of the comprehensive follow-up exam (Step 28), focusing on what you observe while the patient is wearing their lenses.

Key Assessment Areas (Lens ON):

  1. Lens Fit (Re-assessment):
    • Soft Lenses: Confirm good Centration, Coverage, and Movement (C,C,M). Check for edge fluting (loose) or conjunctival indentation/vessel blanching (tight). (Covered in Day 5)
    • RGP Lenses: Re-assess fluorescein pattern (dynamic and static) and lens movement. Check for decentration, excessive bearing, or inadequate edge lift. (Covered in Day 5)
  2. Lens Surface Condition:
    • Deposits: Look for protein films (whitish, hazy), lipid globules (greasy), jelly bumps (mucous/calcium), makeup. Use diffuse and focal illumination. Deposits reduce comfort, vision, Dk/t, and increase GPC risk.
    • Wettability: Does the tear film spread evenly over the lens surface or break up quickly (hydrophobic spots)? Poor wettability causes dryness and blurry vision.
    • Lens Defects: Scratches, chips, tears (especially soft lenses), crazing (fine cracks in RGPs). Defective lenses must be replaced.
  3. Corneal Health (signs visible with lens on):
    • Corneal Edema: Look for vertical striae (fine whitish lines in posterior stroma, visible with parallelepiped or optic section if >5-8% edema). Epithelial microcysts (small, refractile dots) indicate chronic hypoxia. Sattler's veil (epithelial haze reported by patient).
    • Limbal Hyperemia (Redness): Assess degree and location. Can indicate tight fit, hypoxia, solution reaction, or inflammation.
    • Neovascularization: Check limbal area for blood vessel encroachment onto cornea (best seen with lens off, but initial signs may be visible).
  4. Tear Film Over Lens: Assess quality and quantity of pre-lens tear film. Rapid tear break-up time (TBUT) over the lens indicates potential wettability issues or dry eye.
Study Tip - Slit Lamp Routine: Develop a systematic routine for your slit lamp exam (both lens on and lens off) to ensure you don't miss anything. E.g., Lids -> Conjunctiva -> Lens Surface -> Lens Fit -> Underlying Cornea (as much as visible) -> Limbal Area.

Step 30: Cosmetic Contact Lenses

Lenses worn primarily to change the appearance of the eye color or look.

Types:

  • Opaque Lenses: Have a non-transparent colored pattern to completely change eye color (e.g., brown to blue). Have a clear central pupil.
  • Enhancer Tints / Handling Tints: Translucent tint to enhance existing eye color or make lenses easier to see for handling (usually very light blue/green). Do not significantly change eye color.

Motivations for Wear:

  • Fashion, novelty, matching outfits, special occasions.
  • Theatrical or character portrayal.
  • Prosthetic use (to mask corneal scars or iris abnormalities - these are often custom).

Fitting Considerations:

  • Proper Fit is ESSENTIAL: Even though "cosmetic," they are still medical devices. All standard fitting principles apply (Centration, Coverage, Movement).
  • Pupil Size Matching: The clear optic zone of an opaque lens must be large enough to cover the patient's pupil in various lighting conditions to avoid vision being obscured by the colored part.
  • Centration: Critical for opaque lenses. If the lens decenters, the colored iris pattern may not align with the patient's natural iris, or the clear pupil zone may not cover the patient's pupil, leading to an unnatural look and/or visual obstruction.
  • Color/Pattern Selection: Subjective. Patient preference is key, but ECP can advise on what looks natural.
  • Material & Dk/t: Many cosmetic lenses are older hydrogel materials with lower Dk/t. The pigments can also reduce oxygen transmission. Counsel on appropriate wear times to minimize hypoxia.
Risks of Improperly Fitted/Illegal Cosmetic Lenses:
  • Corneal abrasions, infections (Microbial Keratitis), hypoxia, allergic reactions.
  • Often sold illegally online or in novelty stores without a prescription or proper fitting.
  • Public health concern due to high rates of complications with illegally sold lenses.
  • Educate patients that ALL contact lenses, including cosmetic ones, are medical devices requiring a prescription and professional fitting.

Step 31: Fitting Contact Lenses in Children

Requires special considerations for indications, patient/parent cooperation, and lens selection.

Common Indications:

  • Optical Correction:
    • Congenital/Pediatric Aphakia: Following cataract surgery. CLs are vital for visual development.
    • High Anisometropia: Significant difference in Rx between eyes. CLs minimize aniseikonia.
    • High Myopia/Hyperopia.
  • Myopia Control: A rapidly growing indication. Specialized soft multifocal or orthokeratology lenses to slow myopia progression.
  • Amblyopia Treatment (sometimes as part of occlusion therapy or penalization).
  • Therapeutic (e.g., bandage lens after trauma).
  • Cosmetic/Prosthetic (e.g., aniridia, albinism).

Key Considerations:

  • Age: No strict age limit. Depends on child's maturity, motivation, and parental support. Infants can be fitted for aphakia.
  • Parental Involvement & Motivation: CRUCIAL. Parents are often responsible for I&R and care, especially in younger children. They must be committed and competent.
  • Child's Motivation & Cooperation: Older children need to want to wear lenses.
  • Lens Selection:
    • Daily Disposables: Often ideal for hygiene, convenience, reduced risk of solution sensitivities or deposit issues. Good for intermittent wear or sports.
    • Silicone Hydrogels: Preferred for higher oxygen if frequent replacement or extended wear (for aphakia) is needed.
    • RGPs: Excellent optics, durable. Often used for aphakia, keratoconus, or high astigmatism in children.
  • Handling Challenges: Smaller eyes, smaller palpebral apertures, fear/anxiety. Requires patience, positive reinforcement, specialized techniques.
  • Follow-up: Often more frequent than for adults, especially initially, to monitor compliance, ocular health, and refractive changes (children's Rx can change quickly).
  • Communication: Age-appropriate language for the child, detailed explanations for parents.
Analogy - Fitting Children: Like being a pediatric dentist. You need technical skill, but also excellent "chair-side manner" for both the child and the parents, plus a lot of patience and encouragement.

Step 32: Toric Contact Lenses (Soft & RGP)

Used to correct significant astigmatism that cannot be adequately managed with spherical lenses (typically when Residual Astigmatism with a spherical lens is >0.75 D - 1.00 D).

Soft Toric Contact Lenses:

Key Challenge: Rotational Stability. A soft toric lens must orient consistently on the eye for the cylindrical correction to be effective. If it rotates, vision becomes blurry.
  • Stabilization Methods:
    • Prism Ballast: Lens is thicker at the bottom (base-down prism) causing it to orient due to gravity and lid forces. Watermelon seed principle.
    • Peri-Ballast: Similar to prism ballast but the prism is only in the peripheral carrier zone, not the optic zone.
    • Truncation: Bottom edge of lens is slightly flattened. Rarely used in modern soft torics.
    • Dynamic Stabilization (Thin Zones / Double Slab-Off): Lens is thinner at the top and bottom, thicker horizontally. Lid pressure during blinks helps orient and stabilize it. Common in many modern designs.
  • Assessing Fit & Orientation:
    • Standard soft lens fit assessment (C,C,M).
    • Check for Lens Rotation: Most toric lenses have orientation markings (e.g., a small line or dots at 3, 6, and/or 9 o'clock, or just at 6 o'clock). Observe these with the slit lamp.
      • Ideally, no rotation or stable rotation within ~5-10 degrees.
      • If rotated, note direction (clockwise/counter-clockwise from your perspective) and amount (e.g., "10 degrees clockwise").
    • LARS Rule for Over-Refraction: If lens is rotated, adjust the spectacle Rx cylinder axis: Left Add, Right Subtract. (If lens marking rotates to ECP's left, ADD the rotation to the Rx axis. If it rotates to ECP's right, SUBTRACT).
    • Assess visual stability with blinks.

RGP Toric Contact Lenses:

Used when spherical RGPs leave significant Residual Astigmatism (i.e., internal/lenticular astigmatism is >0.75-1.00D).

  • Front Surface Toric (FST) RGP:
    • Spherical back surface, toric front surface (cylinder ground on front).
    • Used for correcting internal/lenticular astigmatism when corneal astigmatism is minimal (<0.75D) or when a spherical RGP already fits well.
    • Requires prism ballast or truncation for stabilization (like soft torics) to prevent rotation.
  • Back Surface Toric (BST) RGP:
    • Toric back surface (to align with a significantly toric cornea), spherical front surface.
    • Used when corneal astigmatism is high (e.g., >2.00-2.50D) and a spherical RGP results in excessive rocking or poor centration.
    • The toric back surface improves fit and stability. The tear lens formed often corrects some, but not all, of the astigmatism. Often induces some astigmatism due to different refractive indices of lens and tears.
  • Bitoric (BiT) RGP:
    • Toric back surface AND toric front surface. The "gold standard" for high corneal astigmatism with significant internal astigmatism.
    • Allows independent correction of corneal topography (with back surface) and total refractive astigmatism (with front surface).
    • SPE (Spherical Power Effect): Back surface toricity induces astigmatism equal to approx. 1/3 of its value * (n'-n), where n' is lens RI and n is tear RI. Front surface cylinder compensates for this and any residual astigmatism. Rotational stability is excellent due to the back surface alignment.
    • CPE (Cylinder Power Effect): Back surface toricity is designed to correct the corneal astigmatism, front surface adds any further needed cylinder. More complex.
Study Tip - Toric Decision Tree: Create a simple flowchart:
1. Is total astigmatism >0.75D?
Yes -> Consider toric. No -> Spherical likely okay.
2. If Yes: Is it mostly corneal or internal? (Compare Spec Rx cyl to K's cyl)
Mostly Corneal -> Spherical RGP or Soft Toric.
Mostly Internal (with low corneal cyl) -> Soft Toric or FST RGP.
Significant Corneal AND Internal -> Bitoric RGP or Soft Toric.

Toric Scenario: A patient has a spectacle Rx of -2.00 / -2.50 x 090. K-readings are 42.00 @ 180 / 42.50 @ 090 (0.50D WTR corneal astigmatism). You fit a spherical soft lens and the over-refraction shows -0.25 / -2.25 x 090.
1. Why is vision likely poor with the spherical soft lens?
2. What type of contact lens would be most appropriate next?

Answer & Rationale

1. Vision is poor because the spherical soft lens does not correct the astigmatism. The residual astigmatism is -2.25 D, which is very significant. The spectacle cylinder is -2.50 D, and the cornea only has 0.50 D of astigmatism. This means most of the astigmatism (~2.00 D) is internal/lenticular.
2. The most appropriate next lens would be a Soft Toric Contact Lens. A spherical RGP would correct the 0.50 D of corneal astigmatism but would leave approximately -2.00 D of internal astigmatism uncorrected. A Front Surface Toric (FST) RGP could also be an option if the patient preferred RGPs, as it's designed for low corneal astigmatism with significant internal astigmatism.
