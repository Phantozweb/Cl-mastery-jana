Detailed Quick Recall Notes: Contact Lens Steps 18-25

Step 18: Effects of Parameter Changes in Spherical Soft Contact Lenses

Sagittal Depth (Sag) Base Curve (BC) Diameter (Dia) Tight Fit Loose Fit

Understanding Sagittal Depth (Sag):

  • Sagittal Depth is the primary determinant of soft lens fit. It's the perpendicular distance from the lens edge plane to the back apex.
  • Increased Sag = Tighter Fit (lens vaults more, sits tighter).
  • Decreased Sag = Looser Fit (lens vaults less, sits looser).

Impact of Parameter Changes on Fit:

Parameter ChangeEffect on Sagittal DepthResulting Fit ChangeWhen to Apply
Steepen Base Curve (BC)
(e.g., 8.6mm → 8.3mm)		 Increases Sag Tighter Fit If current lens is too loose (excessive movement, decentration).
Flatten Base Curve (BC)
(e.g., 8.3mm → 8.6mm)		 Decreases Sag Looser Fit If current lens is too tight (no movement, conjunctival indentation).
Increase Diameter (Dia)
(keeping BC same)		 Increases Sag Tighter Fit (also improves corneal coverage). If lens is loose AND/OR has poor coverage.
Decrease Diameter (Dia)
(keeping BC same)		 Decreases Sag Looser Fit If lens is tight and edge is impinging, or if OAD is clearly too large for HVID. (Less common to change Dia alone for fit, usually BC first).
Analogy - Soft Lens Sag like a Baseball Cap: "Imagine a baseball cap. The Sagittal Depth is how deep the cap is from its brim to the top.
  • A 'steeper BC' is like making the cap's crown more curved and deeper – it will fit tighter.
  • A 'flatter BC' is like making the crown less curved and shallower – it will fit looser.
  • A 'larger diameter' (wider cap) on the same curve will also make it feel tighter because it covers more of your head."
Takeaway: To adjust soft lens fit, manipulate BC and/or Diameter to alter the sagittal depth. Steeper BC or larger Dia tightens the fit; flatter BC or smaller Dia loosens it. Always consider manufacturer guidelines.
Self-Check: A soft lens shows 1.5mm of movement on blink and inferior decentration. To tighten the fit, would you choose a trial lens with a numerically larger or smaller BC value (in mm)?

To tighten the fit (increase sagittal depth), you would choose a trial lens with a numerically smaller BC value (e.g., go from 8.7mm to 8.4mm), which represents a steeper curve. Alternatively, you could increase the diameter.

Step 19: Fitting Spherical RGP Contact Lenses (Low Dk materials)

Fluorescein Sodium Cobalt Blue Light Apical Clearance Apical Touch Alignment Edge Lift Dynamic Fit Static Fit

Fluorescein Pattern Assessment: The Key to RGP Fitting

  • Fluorescein Sodium: Yellow-orange dye, glows bright green under cobalt blue light.
  • Highlights tear layer thickness between RGP lens and cornea:
    • Bright Green Pool: Thick tear layer = Clearance (lens further from cornea).
    • Dark/Black Area: Thin/no tear layer = Touch/Bearing (lens close to/on cornea).
    • Even, Thin Green Sheen: Alignment (lens closely follows corneal curvature).
  • Observe both Static Fit (lens settled) and Dynamic Fit (movement with blink, lens position after blink).

Common RGP Fitting Philosophies & Ideal Fluorescein Patterns:

Fitting PhilosophyApical (Central) PatternMid-Peripheral PatternPeripheral Edge PatternMovement & Centration
Alignment Fit (Lid-Attached or Interpalpebral) Thin, even alignment OR minimal (<0.50D) clearance. Good alignment. Adequate band (~0.5-1.0mm wide) of edge clearance/lift for tear exchange. 1-1.5mm smooth movement; good centration.
Slightly Steep Fit (Apical Clearance) Definite central pool of clearance ("bull's eye"). May show some bearing/touch. Adequate edge clearance. May have slightly less movement; good centration.
Slightly Flat Fit (Apical Touch/Bearing) (Generally Undesirable) Central touch/bearing (dark area). Excessive clearance ("moat"). May have excessive edge lift. Often excessive movement; poor centration (rides high/low). Risk of corneal abrasion.
Analogy - Fluorescein like "Glow-in-the-Dark Water" Under a Glass Plate on a Mold: "Imagine placing a clear glass plate (RGP lens) on a slightly curved clay mold (cornea). You pour glow-in-the-dark water (fluorescein-tinged tears) between them.
  • Where the plate touches the mold, no water glows (dark).
  • Where there's a perfect thin gap, a little water glows evenly.
  • Where there's a big gap (plate lifted), lots of water glows brightly."
Takeaway: RGP fitting is an art and science relying on interpreting fluorescein patterns to achieve an optimal lens-cornea relationship for health, comfort, and vision. Alignment is often the goal.
Self-Check: You observe an RGP lens with a central dark area (no fluorescein) surrounded by a bright green ring in the mid-periphery, and significant edge lift. What type of fit does this indicate?

This indicates a Flat Fit (Apical Touch/Bearing). The lens is resting heavily on the corneal apex, causing excessive clearance in the mid-periphery and periphery.

Step 20: Fitting Spherical RGP Contact Lenses (High Dk materials)

High Dk RGP Oxygen Benefits Material Flexure Wettability Deposit Resistance

Advantages of High Dk RGP Materials:

  • Significantly Increased Oxygen Transmission (Dk/t): Reduces risk of corneal hypoxia and related complications (edema, neovascularization).
  • Better for extended wear considerations (if specifically approved and managed).
  • Potentially better for compromised corneas or patients with higher oxygen demands.

Potential Challenges & Fitting Considerations with Some High Dk RGPs:

  • Material Flexure: Some higher Dk materials (especially those with higher silicone content) can be less rigid and may bend or "flex" on an astigmatic cornea.
    • This flexure can induce unpredicted residual astigmatism in the over-refraction.
    • Check with keratometry over the lens (on eye) or by observing changes in ORx if flexure is suspected.
    • May require a thicker lens design or a different material if flexure is problematic.
  • Surface Wettability: Silicone is inherently hydrophobic. Some early high Dk Si-containing RGPs had wettability challenges.
    • Modern high Dk FSAs (Fluoro-Silicone Acrylates) generally have good wettability due to fluorine component and manufacturing processes.
    • Plasma surface treatments are often applied to enhance initial wettability.
    • Proper lens care and compatible solutions are important.
  • Deposit Resistance: Generally good with FSA materials, but can vary. Silicone content can sometimes attract lipid deposits if not cleaned well.
  • Durability/Brittleness: Some very high Dk materials might be slightly more prone to chipping or breaking compared to lower Dk, more robust materials. Handle with care.

Fitting Approach:

  • Fluorescein pattern assessment principles remain the same as for low Dk RGPs.
  • Be particularly mindful of potential flexure during over-refraction, especially on corneas with >1.50D of astigmatism.
  • Select appropriate care systems specifically recommended for the high Dk material being used.
Takeaway: High Dk RGP materials offer significant oxygen benefits but require awareness of potential material-specific issues like flexure and historical wettability concerns (largely improved now). The fundamental fitting assessment using fluorescein remains unchanged.
Self-Check: If a high Dk RGP lens is flexing on an astigmatic cornea, how might this manifest during the over-refraction?

It might manifest as an unexpected amount or axis of astigmatism in the over-refraction that doesn't correlate well with the expected residual (internal) astigmatism. The astigmatic correction in the ORx might also be unstable or variable. Performing keratometry over the lens can help confirm flexure by showing induced toricity on the front surface of the RGP.

SAM-FAP Rule (for BOZR changes & maintaining lens effectivity):
  • Steeper Add Minus (to the lens power). Why? Steeper BC creates a more plus-powered tear lens, so lens power needs more minus to compensate.
  • Flatter Add Plus (to the lens power). Why? Flatter BC creates a more minus-powered tear lens, so lens power needs more plus.
  • Rule of thumb: 0.05mm BC change ≈ 0.25D power compensation.
Takeaway: Modifying RGP parameters allows fine-tuning of the lens-cornea relationship. Understanding how each change affects the fluorescein pattern, lens movement, and potentially the required power is crucial for successful RGP fitting and troubleshooting.
Self-Check: An RGP lens is fitting too steep centrally (excessive apical clearance) and has minimal edge lift. Name two parameter changes you could make to improve this fit.

To improve this steep fit with minimal edge lift:

  1. Flatten the BOZR: This will reduce the apical clearance and bring the central part of the lens closer to the cornea.
  2. Flatten the Peripheral Curve Radii (PCRs) and/or Increase Peripheral Curve Widths (PCWs) of the flatter curves: This will increase the peripheral edge clearance/lift, allowing better tear exchange.
You might also consider a slightly smaller OZD if the steepness is confined to a very large central zone.

Step 22: Understanding Astigmatism & Basic Correction Options

Corneal Astigmatism Internal/Lenticular Astigmatism Total Astigmatism Residual Astigmatism (RA) Spherical Soft CL Spherical RGP CL Lacrimal Lens Toric CL

Types of Astigmatism:

  • Corneal Astigmatism: Due to toricity of the anterior corneal surface. Measured by Keratometry/Topography.
  • Internal/Lenticular Astigmatism: Due to toricity/tilt of crystalline lens, or posterior corneal toricity. Not directly measured by K's.
    • Calculated: Total Astigmatism (refraction) - Corneal Astigmatism (K's) ≈ Internal Astig. (approximate).
  • Total Astigmatism: Measured by subjective refraction (in spectacle Rx). Combination of corneal and internal.

Residual Astigmatism (RA) with Spherical CLs:

  • RA is the astigmatism remaining uncorrected when a spherical CL is worn.
  • With Spherical Soft CL: Soft lens drapes. RA ≈ Total Astigmatism.
    • Spherical soft CLs may "mask" or be tolerated for small amounts of total astigmatism (typically ≤0.75D, maybe up to 1.00D depending on visual needs). If RA >0.75-1.00D, vision is usually compromised, Toric Soft CL needed.
  • With Spherical RGP CL: The Lacrimal Lens (tear lens) neutralizes most of the Corneal Astigmatism. Therefore, RA ≈ Internal/Lenticular Astigmatism.
    • If RA (i.e., internal astig.) >0.75-1.00D, then a Toric RGP CL needed (e.g., Front Surface Toric, Bitoric).
Analogy - Correcting a Warped Window Frame: "Imagine your eye's 'window frame' (cornea + lens) is warped (Total Astigmatism).
  • The Corneal Astigmatism is a warp just in the outer glass pane.
  • The Internal Astigmatism is a warp in the inner frame or inner glass pane.
  • A Spherical Soft CL is like a flexible film; it just follows all the warps, so you still see the full warp (RA ≈ Total).
  • A Spherical RGP CL is like putting a perfectly flat, rigid piece of glass over the outer warped pane. The 'tear gap' fills in the outer warp. You only see the 'inner frame warp' (RA ≈ Internal)."
Takeaway: Understanding the source of astigmatism (corneal vs. internal) is crucial for selecting the appropriate CL type. Spherical RGPs excel at correcting corneal astigmatism due to the tear lens. Toric CLs are needed when significant astigmatism remains uncorrected by spherical lenses.
Self-Check: A patient has K-readings: 42.00 @180 / 44.00 @090. Spectacle Rx: -3.00 / -2.00 x 180. If you fit a spherical RGP, what is the approximate expected residual astigmatism?

Corneal Astigmatism = 44.00D - 42.00D = 2.00D (WTR, axis approx 090 for minus cyl form, or 180 for plus cyl form of K's). Spectacle (Total) Astigmatism = 2.00D (ATR, axis 180). Since the corneal astigmatism (2.00D WTR) and total astigmatism (2.00D ATR) are equal in magnitude but roughly opposite in axis type (one effectively cancels the other when considering the internal component), the expected residual astigmatism with a spherical RGP would be minimal (close to 0D) if the RGP fully corrects the corneal toricity. However, if we convert K-cyl to match spectacle cyl axis (e.g., K-cyl -2.00 x 180), then RA = Total Cyl - Corneal Cyl = (-2.00) - (-2.00) = 0 D.
More simply: The RGP corrects the 2.00D of corneal astigmatism. The spectacle Rx *also* has 2.00D of astigmatism. This implies the internal astigmatism is very low or zero. So, RA ≈ 0D.

Step 23: Fitting Contact Lenses in Aphakia

Aphakia High Plus Power Center Thickness (CT) Lens Mass Lenticulation High Dk Materials Pediatric Aphakia

Understanding Aphakia:

  • Aphakia: Absence of the crystalline lens of the eye.
    • Common causes: Surgical removal (e.g., cataracts without IOL implant), congenital absence, trauma.
  • Results in very high hyperopia (typically +10.00D to +20.00D or more).

Challenges & Design Considerations for Aphakic CLs:

  • High Plus Powers: Lead to:
    • Thick Center Thickness (CT): Significantly reduces Dk/t, increasing hypoxic risk.
    • Increased Lens Mass/Weight: Can cause lens decentration (especially inferiorly) and instability.
  • Optic Zone Diameter (OZD): Aphakic pupils can be large or irregular; OZD must be sufficient but larger OZD further increases CT/mass.
  • Lenticulation (primarily for Soft Lenses): The peripheral portion of a high plus lens is thinned down (a "minus carrier" design) to:
    • Reduce overall lens mass and thickness.
    • Improve comfort by thinning the edge profile.
    • Aid in centration and stability.
  • Material Choice: High Dk Materials are ESSENTIAL (Silicone Hydrogels for soft; high Dk FSAs for RGPs) to maximize oxygen due to increased thickness.
  • Patient Profile & Handling:
    • Often elderly or very young (infants with congenital aphakia).
    • May have reduced dexterity, poor vision without correction, making handling difficult. Parental involvement is crucial for pediatric cases.
    • Other ocular conditions may co-exist (e.g., post-surgical corneal changes, glaucoma, amblyopia).

Fitting Approach:

  • Soft Lenses: Utilize specialized aphakic designs with lenticulation. Careful assessment of centration, movement, and physiological response due to thickness/weight. Extended wear may be considered for infants.
  • RGP Lenses: Can provide excellent, stable optics. Smaller OADs might be used to reduce mass. High Dk materials. Can correct significant post-surgical astigmatism.
Analogy - Aphakic CL like a Very Strong Magnifying Glass: "Imagine needing an extremely powerful magnifying glass (high plus CL) to see. This 'magnifier' would naturally be very thick in the middle. The challenge is to make it wearable:
  • Lenticulation is like shaving down the thick edges of the magnifier so it's not too bulky and heavy on your eye.
  • It needs to be made of 'super breathable' material (High Dk) because the thickness blocks air."
Takeaway: Aphakic CL fitting is a specialized area primarily addressing the challenges of high plus powers, requiring careful lens design (lenticulation), high Dk materials, and attention to patient handling capabilities.
Self-Check: Why is a "minus carrier" design (lenticulation) often used in high plus power soft contact lenses for aphakia?

A minus carrier design thins the periphery of the otherwise very thick high plus lens. This reduces the overall mass and thickness of the lens, improving comfort by providing a thinner edge profile, and aiding in better centration and stability on the eye, as a very heavy lens would tend to decenter inferiorly.

Step 24: Fitting Contact Lenses in Pseudophakia

Pseudophakia Intraocular Lens (IOL) Residual Refractive Error Post-operative Astigmatism Cataract Surgery

Understanding Pseudophakia:

  • Pseudophakia: The condition where the eye's natural crystalline lens has been surgically removed (typically during cataract surgery) and replaced with an artificial Intraocular Lens (IOL).

Reasons for CL Wear in Pseudophakic Patients:

  • Correction of Residual Refractive Error:
    • The IOL power calculation may not have resulted in perfect emmetropia (no refractive error).
    • Patient may be left with some myopia, hyperopia, or astigmatism.
  • Correction of Post-operative Astigmatism: Surgically induced astigmatism (SIA) from incisions, or pre-existing astigmatism not fully corrected by a toric IOL (if used).
  • Anisometropia: If only one eye had surgery, or IOL powers/outcomes differ significantly between eyes, leading to spectacle intolerance.
  • Presbyopia Management: If a monofocal IOL was implanted (correcting for distance only), patient will still need near correction. Multifocal CLs or monovision CLs can be options.
  • Patient Preference: Desire for spectacle independence for cosmetic reasons, sports, or lifestyle.

CL Fitting Considerations:

  • Refractive Powers: Usually much lower and within the "normal" range compared to aphakia. Standard CL designs (spherical, toric, multifocal) are often suitable.
  • Corneal Condition:
    • Assess for any post-surgical changes: incision integrity, corneal edema (usually resolved), endothelial cell status (especially if surgery was complicated).
    • Check for pre-existing or surgically induced dry eye, which is common after cataract surgery.
  • IOL Type & Position: Most modern IOLs are well-centered and stable. Knowledge of IOL type (monofocal, multifocal, toric) helps understand patient's visual status and expectations.
  • Timing of Fit: Generally wait until the eye is stable, inflammation has resolved, and refraction is stable post-surgery (e.g., 4-8 weeks, or as advised by the surgeon).
  • Overall Ocular Health: Consider any co-existing conditions (e.g., glaucoma, macular degeneration).
Analogy - Pseudophakia like Getting a Lens Implant in a Camera: "After cataract surgery with an IOL, it's like your eye-camera got a new internal lens. Sometimes, this new lens isn't perfectly focused for all distances, or it might leave a little blur (Residual Rx Error). A contact lens then acts like a fine-tuning filter placed on the *outside* of the camera to sharpen the image perfectly."
Takeaway: Fitting CLs on pseudophakic eyes is generally more straightforward than aphakic fitting, primarily addressing residual refractive errors or astigmatism. Careful assessment of the post-surgical ocular surface and patient visual needs is key.
Self-Check: A patient had cataract surgery with a monofocal IOL set for good distance vision. They are now unhappy needing reading glasses. What type of contact lens could be considered to help them with presbyopia?

Several CL options could be considered:

  1. Multifocal Contact Lenses: These could provide simultaneous distance and near vision.
  2. Monovision: Fitting one eye (usually dominant) with a distance CL and the other eye (non-dominant) with a near CL.
  3. Simply wearing reading glasses over their distance single vision CLs if they only need near help occasionally.
The choice would depend on patient motivation, visual needs, and trial lens success.

Step 25: Fitting in Keratoconus (Introduction)

Keratoconus (KC) Corneal Ectasia Irregular Astigmatism RGP Lenses Three-Point Touch Apical Clearance Specialty Lenses (Hybrid, Scleral) Vogt's Striae Fleischer's Ring Munson's Sign

Understanding Keratoconus (KC):

  • A progressive, non-inflammatory corneal ectasia (thinning and bulging) where the cornea takes on a cone-like shape.
  • Typically bilateral, often asymmetrical. Onset usually in puberty/young adulthood.
  • Causes significant irregular astigmatism and myopia, leading to blurred/distorted vision, glare, halos, monocular diplopia.

Key Clinical Signs:

  • Slit Lamp: Vogt's Striae (vertical stress lines in deep stroma), Fleischer's Ring (iron deposit at cone base), corneal thinning (especially at apex), apical scarring (advanced).
  • External Observation: Munson's Sign (V-shaped lower lid on downgaze).
  • Retinoscopy: Scissoring reflex.
  • Keratometry/Topography: Steep K's, high astigmatism, inferior steepening, skewed axes. Topography is crucial for diagnosis, staging, and monitoring.

Contact Lenses for KC - Why & How:

  • Spectacles often fail to provide adequate vision due to the irregular nature of the astigmatism.
  • Rigid Gas Permeable (RGP) Lenses are the primary visual rehabilitation tool:
    • The rigid lens vaults over the irregular cornea.
    • The tear lens formed between the RGP and cornea fills in the irregularities, creating a smooth, regular anterior optical surface.

Basic RGP Fitting Philosophies for KC (Introductory):

PhilosophyDescriptionGoalFluorescein Pattern Indication
Three-Point Touch (Classic, for mild/moderate KC) Lens lightly touches corneal apex and bears on two opposing mid-peripheral points. Distribute lens weight, stabilize lens over cone. Light apical touch (dark), mid-peripheral bearing (dark/thin green), adequate peripheral clearance.
Apical Clearance / Vaulting (Modern, esp. for moderate/advanced or sensitive cones) Lens designed to completely clear (vault over) the corneal apex, landing on healthier mid-peripheral/peripheral cornea. Minimize mechanical stress on fragile cone apex, reduce risk of scarring. Definite central apical clearance (green pool), landing zone shows alignment/light bearing, good peripheral clearance.

Other Lens Options for KC (Brief Mention):

  • Specialty Keratoconic RGP Designs: Multicurve, aspheric, reverse geometry, variable OZD.
  • Hybrid Lenses: RGP center (optics) + soft peripheral skirt (comfort/stability).
  • Scleral Lenses: Large diameter lenses that vault the entire cornea, resting on the sclera. Excellent for advanced/very irregular KC, or when other lenses fail.
  • Piggybacking: Soft lens under an RGP for comfort. Custom soft toric lenses for very mild KC (rarely sufficient).
Analogy - Fitting KC with an RGP like Bridging a Pothole: "Imagine the keratoconic cornea is a road with a big, pointy pothole (the cone).
  • A Three-Point Touch RGP is like a stiff board that rests lightly on the tip of the pothole and two spots on the road beside it.
  • An Apical Clearance RGP is like a strong bridge built completely *over* the pothole, with its supports resting on the solid road on either side, not touching the weak spot at all."
Takeaway: Keratoconus requires specialized contact lens fitting, most often with RGPs, to provide functional vision by masking corneal irregularity. Fitting aims to optimize vision, comfort, and long-term corneal health, often involving custom lens designs.
Self-Check: What is Munson's sign and what condition does it indicate?

Munson's sign is a V-shaped indentation or protrusion of the lower eyelid when the patient looks downwards. It is caused by the conical shape of the cornea in Keratoconus pushing against the lid margin.
