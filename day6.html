Step 21: Effects of RGP CL Parameter Changes on Lens Fitting

Knowing how to modify an RGP design to improve the fit based on fluorescein pattern and lens behavior.

SAM - FAP Rule (for BOZR changes and power):
  • Steeper Add Minus (If you make the BOZR steeper, you add minus power to the lens to compensate for the change in the tear lens power).
  • Flatter Add Plus (If you make the BOZR flatter, you add plus power).
  • This primarily applies when trying to maintain the same overall refractive correction after a BC change.
Parameter Changed How it's Changed Effect on Fluorescein Pattern & Fit Impact on Power (if maintaining overall Rx)
Back Optic Zone Radius (BOZR) / Base Curve (BC) Steepen (e.g., 7.8mm to 7.7mm) Increases central/apical clearance (more green centrally). Tightens overall fit. Reduces movement. Add Minus (SAM)
Flatten (e.g., 7.7mm to 7.8mm) Decreases central/apical clearance (more touch centrally). Loosens overall fit. Increases movement. Add Plus (FAP)
Back Optic Zone Diameter (BOZD) / Optic Zone Diameter (OZD) Increase Enlarges central fitting zone. May increase apical clearance if BOZR is steep relative to cornea. Can improve centration if too small. Minimal direct power effect, but affects sag.
Decrease Reduces central fitting zone. May increase apical touch if BOZR is flat. Peripheral curves start sooner. Minimal direct power effect, but affects sag.
Peripheral Curve Radius (PCR) Steepen (smaller mm value) Reduces peripheral edge clearance/lift. Tightens peripheral fit. No direct effect on central power.
Flatten (larger mm value) Increases peripheral edge clearance/lift. Loosens peripheral fit. No direct effect on central power.
Peripheral Curve Width (PCW) Increase Widens the area of that specific peripheral curve. Pushes subsequent curves further out. No direct effect on central power.
Decrease Narrows the area of that specific peripheral curve. Allows subsequent curves to start sooner. No direct effect on central power.
Overall Diameter (OAD) Increase Increases sagittal depth (tighter fit if BC unchanged). May improve centration (especially for lid-attached fits) or coverage. Can affect centration and stability.
Decrease Decreases sagittal depth (looser fit if BC unchanged). May be needed if OAD is too large causing impingement or excessive lid interaction. Can affect centration and stability.
Study Tip - RGP Troubleshooting: Imagine an RGP lens that drops low and has central apical touch.
1. What type of fit is this indicating centrally? (Flat)
2. What change to the BOZR would you make? (Steepen)
3. What change to OAD might help with a low-riding lens? (Slightly increase if lid interaction desired, or sometimes decrease if too heavy).

Step 22: Understanding Astigmatism & Basic Correction Options

Astigmatism is when the eye doesn't focus light evenly due to an irregularly shaped cornea or lens.

Types of Astigmatism:

  • Corneal Astigmatism: Caused by the cornea being shaped more like a football (toric) than a basketball (spherical). Measured by Keratometry or Topography.
  • Internal/Lenticular Astigmatism: Caused by the crystalline lens inside the eye being tilted or toric. Cannot be measured directly by K's.
  • Total Astigmatism: The astigmatism measured during subjective refraction (what's in the spectacle Rx). It's the sum of corneal and internal astigmatism.
  • Residual Astigmatism (RA): The astigmatism that remains uncorrected when a spherical contact lens is placed on an astigmatic eye.
    • With a spherical soft CL, RA ≈ Total Astigmatism (because soft lenses drape and don't significantly correct corneal astigmatism).
    • With a spherical RGP CL, RA ≈ Internal/Lenticular Astigmatism (because the RGP's tear lens corrects most of the corneal astigmatism).

Basic Correction Options with Spherical Lenses:

  • Spherical Soft Contact Lenses:
    • Generally, can "mask" or tolerate small amounts of total astigmatism (typically ≤ 0.75 D, maybe up to 1.00 D if patient is not visually demanding).
    • If RA (which is basically total astigmatism here) is >0.75D - 1.00D, vision will likely be blurry, and a toric soft CL is needed.
  • Spherical RGP Contact Lenses:
    • The lacrimal lens (tear lens) formed under an RGP neutralizes most of the corneal astigmatism.
    • Spherical RGPs are an excellent option if most of the total astigmatism is corneal.
    • If the Residual Astigmatism (i.e., the internal/lenticular astigmatism) is significant (>0.75D - 1.00D), then a toric RGP (front surface toric, back surface toric, or bitoric) may be needed.
Analogy - RGP Tear Lens for Astigmatism: Imagine a bumpy, irregular road surface (astigmatic cornea). An RGP lens is like laying down a perfectly smooth, rigid sheet of glass over it. The "potholes" (irregularities) under the glass fill up with "water" (tears), creating a new, smooth front surface for light to pass through.

Astigmatism Scenario: Spectacle Rx: -3.00 / -1.50 x 180. K-readings: 43.00 @ 180 / 44.50 @ 090.
1. How much corneal astigmatism is there?
2. If you fit a spherical RGP, approximately how much residual astigmatism would you expect? Would a spherical RGP likely work well?

Answer & Rationale

1. Corneal Astigmatism = 44.50 D - 43.00 D = 1.50 D (WTR, as the vertical meridian @090 is steeper).
2. Total Astigmatism (from Rx) = 1.50 D. Corneal Astigmatism = 1.50 D.
Residual Astigmatism (RA) with spherical RGP ≈ Total Astigmatism - Corneal Astigmatism.
RA ≈ 1.50 D - 1.50 D = 0 D (approximately).
Yes, a spherical RGP would likely work very well because almost all the astigmatism is corneal and will be corrected by the tear lens.

Step 23: Fitting Contact Lenses in Aphakia

Aphakia is the absence of the eye's crystalline lens, usually after cataract surgery without an intraocular lens (IOL) implant, or due to trauma/congenital conditions.

Key Challenges & Considerations:

  • Very High Plus Powers Needed: Typically +10.00 D to +20.00 D or even higher.
    • Causes CLs to be very thick centrally, leading to:
  • Lens Design for High Plus Powers:
    • Center Thickness (CT): Significant. Can reduce Dk/t dramatically, leading to hypoxia.
    • Lens Mass/Weight: Heavy lenses tend to decenter inferiorly.
    • Optic Zone Diameter (OZD): Must be large enough due to dilated pupil often seen in aphakia, but larger OZD increases thickness.
    • Lenticulation (for soft lenses): The peripheral part of the lens is thinned down (a "carrier") to reduce overall mass and improve comfort/centration. RGP designs may also use special peripheral curves or blended lenticulars.
  • Material Choice: High Dk materials are ESSENTIAL due to the thickness, especially for soft lenses (Silicone Hydrogels preferred) and RGPs.
  • Patient Profile:
    • Often elderly (post-cataract surgery without IOL) or very young (congenital aphakia).
    • May have handling difficulties (dexterity, vision).
    • May have other ocular conditions (e.g., post-surgical corneal changes, glaucoma).
  • Fitting Approach:
    • Soft Lenses: Specialized aphakic designs. Careful assessment of centration and movement is crucial due to lens weight.
    • RGP Lenses: Can provide excellent optics. Smaller OAD might be used to reduce mass. High Dk materials.

Step 24: Fitting Contact Lenses in Pseudophakia

Pseudophakia is when the natural crystalline lens has been replaced with an artificial Intraocular Lens (IOL), typically during cataract surgery.

CL Fitting Considerations (Generally More Straightforward than Aphakia):

  • Reason for CL Wear:
    • Residual Refractive Error: The IOL power calculation wasn't perfect, leaving the patient myopic, hyperopic, or astigmatic.
    • Anisometropia: If only one eye had surgery, or IOL powers differ significantly.
    • Patient preference (e.g., for sports, don't want to wear glasses).
  • Refractive Powers: Usually much lower and more "normal" range than in aphakia. Standard CL designs are often suitable.
  • Corneal Condition: Assess for any post-surgical changes, dry eye, or astigmatism induced by incisions. Corneal topography can be useful.
  • IOL Type: Most IOLs don't significantly affect CL fitting, but awareness of multifocal IOLs is important if patient complains of visual disturbances.
  • Timing of Fit: Usually wait for the eye to be stable post-surgery (e.g., 4-8 weeks, or as advised by surgeon).
Fitting CLs on pseudophakic eyes is often similar to fitting a "phakic" (normal lens) eye, but with careful attention to the post-surgical ocular surface and any residual refractive error.
