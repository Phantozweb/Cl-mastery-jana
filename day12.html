Step 44: Checking Finished Lens Parameters (Lens Verification)

The process of measuring the parameters of a newly received contact lens (especially custom RGPs) to ensure they match what was ordered and meet tolerance standards.

Why Verify?

  • To confirm accuracy of manufacturing. Errors can lead to poor fit, discomfort, or incorrect vision.
  • Essential for custom-made RGP lenses.
  • Helps in troubleshooting fitting problems (is it the lens or the eye?).
  • Ensures consistency if a lens needs to be reordered.

Parameters to Verify & Instruments Used (Primarily for RGPs):

Parameter Instrument(s) Brief How-To / What to Look For
Back Optic Zone Radius (BOZR) / Base Curve Radiuscope (Measures Sagittal Depth) Focus on aerial image of lens surface, then mires reflected from back surface. Difference in readings gives radius.
Back Vertex Power (BVP) Lensometer (Focimeter) Place lens (concave side up for RGP on lens stop). Focus mires. Read power. Check for cylinder in toric lenses.
Center Thickness (CT) Thickness Gauge (Dial or Digital) Carefully place lens under gauge probe.
Overall Diameter (OAD) Measuring Magnifier (V-groove reticle), Profile Analyzer Align lens edges with scale.
Optic Zone Diameter (OZD / BOZD) Measuring Magnifier, Profile Analyzer, Shadowgraph Identify transition between central curve and peripheral curves.
Peripheral Curve Widths (PCW) & Radii (PCR) Measuring Magnifier (can estimate widths), Profile Analyzer, Radiuscope (for radii if distinct enough) More complex to verify accurately. Often assessed qualitatively or via profile.
Edge Contour & Surface Quality Stereomicroscope, Slit Lamp (low mag), Hand Magnifier, Projection Magnifier Smooth, well-polished edge. No chips, scratches, crazing, or residues.

Soft Lens Verification: Primarily involves checking power with a lensometer (can be tricky due to flexibility) and confirming BC/Diameter from packaging against what was ordered. Visual inspection for defects.

Tolerance Standards: ISO (International Organization for Standardization) provides acceptable deviation limits for each parameter (e.g., BOZR +/- 0.05mm, Power +/- 0.25D). Lenses outside tolerance should be rejected/returned.

Study Tip - Instrument Match: For each RGP parameter, firmly link it to the primary instrument used for its verification.

Step 45: Contact Lens Modification of Finished Lenses (RGPs)

Making minor adjustments to RGP lens parameters in-office to improve fit or comfort. This is a skilled procedure requiring specialized equipment.

Common In-Office Modifications:

  • Surface Polishing:
    • To remove light surface deposits or scratches, improve wettability.
    • Uses fine polishing compounds (e.g., Silvo, Xpal) on a soft sponge tool with a lens spinner unit.
    • Can slightly alter lens power (flatten / add plus) if overdone.
  • Edge Polishing / Shaping:
    • To smooth a rough edge, reshape edge profile (e.g., make more rounded or tapered) for comfort.
    • Uses various shaped tools (cone, flat, round) with polishing compounds.
  • Power Adjustment (Minor):
    • Adding small amounts of plus power (by flattening the front surface with specific tools and compounds). Usually up to +0.50D to +0.75D.
    • Adding minus power is more difficult and usually requires sending to lab.
  • Blending Peripheral Curves: Smoothing the transition between different peripheral curves to improve comfort or tear flow.
  • Reducing Overall Diameter (Very Minor): Grinding down the edge carefully.

When is Modification Appropriate?

  • For minor adjustments that can save time and cost of reordering from the lab.
  • When a specific problem (e.g., edge discomfort, slight power issue) is identified that can be addressed by modification.
  • NOT for major parameter changes or if lens is significantly out of tolerance.

Tools & Equipment:

  • Lens modification unit (spinner) with various tool attachments (suction cups, sponge tools, shaped tools).
  • Polishing compounds (different abrasiveness).
  • Radiuscope, lensometer, measuring magnifier for checking changes.
Caution with Modification: It's easy to ruin a lens. Practice on old/discarded lenses first. Always re-verify parameters after modification. Document changes.

Step 46: Instrumentation in Contact Lens Practice (Overview)

A summary of the key instruments used throughout the contact lens fitting and management process.

  • Slit Lamp Biomicroscope: Primary tool for assessing anterior eye health, lens fit, and detecting complications. (Day 1, Day 8)
  • Keratometer: Measures central corneal curvature. Essential for initial lens selection (especially RGP). (Day 3)
  • Corneal Topographer: Maps entire corneal surface. For irregular corneas, keratoconus, post-surgical fitting, advanced RGP design. (Day 4)
  • Radiuscope: Measures RGP lens Base Curve (BOZR) for verification. (Day 12)
  • Lensometer (Focimeter): Measures lens power (spectacles and CLs). (Day 12)
  • Pachymeter: Measures corneal thickness. For edema detection, KC monitoring, pre/post-op. (Day 11)
  • Specular Microscope: Assesses corneal endothelium. For monitoring long-term wearers, pre-EW/CW. (Day 11)
  • Trial Lens Sets:
    • Soft Lens Trial Sets: Range of powers, BCs, Dias for common disposable/FR lenses.
    • RGP Trial Sets: Systematically arranged sets of known parameters (BC, Dia, Power) for diagnostic RGP fitting.
  • Fluorescein Sodium Strips: Dye used with cobalt blue light to assess RGP fit, corneal integrity (staining), TBUT.
  • Burton Lamp / Handheld Cobalt Blue Light & Magnifier: For gross fluorescein pattern assessment if slit lamp not immediately available or for quick checks.
  • Other Measuring Tools: HVID ruler, PD ruler, measuring magnifiers/reticles, thickness gauges.
Analogy - ECP's Toolkit: Like a skilled craftsperson, an ECP has a range of specialized tools, each with a specific purpose, to measure, assess, design, and troubleshoot. Knowing how and when to use each tool is key.

Step 47: Care of Contact Lenses – Deeper Dive into Contact Lens Solutions

Understanding the components and functions of different lens care solutions.

Multipurpose Solutions (MPS) for Soft Lenses: "All-in-One" (but still need to rub!)

Designed to clean, rinse, disinfect, and store soft lenses.

  • Key Components & Functions:
    • Disinfectants/Preservatives: Kill bacteria, fungi, Acanthamoeba. Common agents:
      • PHMB (Polyhexamethylene Biguanide)
      • Polyquaternium-1 (Polyquad™)
      • Aldox™ (Myristamidopropyl Dimethylamine) - Effective against Acanthamoeba.
      • Alexidine
      Can cause sensitivity or SICS in some patients.
    • Surfactants (Cleaners): Loosen and remove debris, deposits (proteins, lipids) from lens surface. (e.g., Poloxamine, Poloxamer).
    • Wetting Agents / Lubricants / Comfort Agents: Enhance lens surface wettability and comfort. (e.g., HPMC, Hyaluronan, Tetronic).
    • Buffers: Maintain pH of solution similar to natural tears for comfort and optimal disinfectant efficacy. (e.g., borate, phosphate, citrate).
    • Chelating Agents (Sequestering Agents): Bind and remove calcium deposits. (e.g., EDTA - Ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid).

Hydrogen Peroxide (H2O2) Based Systems for Soft (and RGP) Lenses:

  • Principle: Use 3% Hydrogen Peroxide for highly effective disinfection. MUST BE NEUTRALIZED before lens insertion.
  • Neutralization Methods:
    • One-Step Systems: Neutralization occurs simultaneously with disinfection in a special case containing a catalytic disc (platinum coated). Fixed soak time (usually ~6 hours).
    • Two-Step Systems (less common now): Separate neutralizing tablet or solution added after disinfection period.
  • Advantages: Excellent disinfection (broad spectrum, including Acanthamoeba cysts). Preservative-free once neutralized - good for patients with solution sensitivities. Effective cleaning.
  • Critical Warning: Unneutralized H2O2 will cause severe stinging, burning, and corneal injury if put directly in the eye. Patient education is vital.

RGP Lens Care Systems: Often separate components.

  • Cleaners: Abrasive or non-abrasive surfactant cleaners for daily rubbing.
  • Soaking/Wetting/Disinfecting Solutions: For storing, disinfecting, and conditioning RGPs to enhance wettability. Contain disinfectants and wetting agents.
  • Enzymatic Cleaners (Protein Removers): Weekly use tablets or liquids to break down stubborn protein deposits (for both soft and RGP, though less common now with frequent replacement soft lenses).

Saline Solutions:

  • Isotonic, buffered salt solution. NO DISINFECTING PROPERTIES.
  • Used for rinsing lenses after cleaning and before insertion (especially with H2O2 systems or if sensitive to MPS for rinsing).
  • Used for filling scleral lenses.
  • Preserved (for multi-use bottles) or unpreserved (single-use vials - preferred for rinsing before insertion).

Solution-Lens Material Compatibility & Patient Sensitivity:

  • Some SiHy materials may interact differently with certain MPS preservatives, potentially leading to SICS or discomfort.
  • Always recommend solutions compatible with the specific lens material being worn.
  • If sensitivity is suspected, switch to a different preservative system or H2O2.
Study Tip - Solution Chart: Create a chart with columns: Solution Type (MPS, H2O2, RGP Cleaner, etc.), Key Active Ingredients, Primary Function, Pros, Cons/Cautions.

Solution Choice: A patient wearing Silicone Hydrogel monthly lenses reports stinging and redness immediately upon inserting their lenses after using their usual MPS. Slit lamp shows diffuse fine corneal staining. What type of care system might you recommend as an alternative, and why?

Answer & Rationale

This sounds like Solution-Induced Corneal Staining (SICS) or a sensitivity to a component (likely a preservative) in their current MPS.
An excellent alternative would be a Hydrogen Peroxide (H2O2) based system.
Why:

  1. Preservative-Free (after neutralization): H2O2 systems, once fully neutralized, result in a gentle, preservative-free saline solution contacting the eye. This eliminates the common cause of SICS and sensitivity reactions linked to MPS preservatives.
  2. Excellent Disinfection: H2O2 provides broad-spectrum disinfection, which is beneficial for monthly lenses.
  3. Good Cleaning: The bubbling action during neutralization can help dislodge debris.
It's crucial to thoroughly educate the patient on the proper use of the H2O2 system, especially the neutralization step, to avoid accidental instillation of unneutralized peroxide.
