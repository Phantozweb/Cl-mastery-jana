Step 5: Glossary of Contact Lens Terms - Part 1 (Basic Parameters)

These are the fundamental "specs" of a contact lens.

Term Abbreviation(s) Definition (Simple Terms) Analogy/Relevance
Base Curve (Radius) BC, BCR The main curvature of the back surface of the lens (the part that touches the eye). Measured in mm. Like the curve of a bowl. A steeper BC (smaller mm number) fits a more curved cornea. A flatter BC (larger mm number) fits a less curved cornea.
Overall Diameter / Total Diameter OAD, TD The total width of the lens from edge to edge. Measured in mm. How big the "plate" is. Soft lenses are generally larger than RGPs.
Optic Zone Diameter / Back Optic Zone Diameter OZD, BOZD The central area of the lens that has the corrective power. The "seeing" part of the lens. Must be larger than the pupil in dim light.
Back Vertex Power BVP, Pwr The refractive power of the lens (e.g., -2.50 D, +4.00 D). Measured in Diopters (D). How strong the lens is for correcting vision.
Center Thickness CT Thickness of the lens at its very center. Measured in mm. Affects Dk/t (oxygen transmission) and lens handling. Minus lenses are thinner centrally, plus lenses thicker.
Oxygen Permeability Dk A material property: how easily oxygen passes through a specific lens material. Higher Dk = more O2. How "breathable" the fabric of the lens is.
Oxygen Transmissibility Dk/t How easily oxygen passes through a specific lens of a certain thickness (t). Dk divided by center thickness (t). Higher Dk/t = more O2 to cornea. How much air gets through the *actual* blanket (material + thickness). This is the more clinically important value for oxygen.
HEMA (Hydroxyethyl Methacrylate) HEMA A common polymer used to make traditional soft hydrogel contact lenses. The "original recipe" for many soft lenses. Oxygen travels through its water content.
Silicone Hydrogel SiHy A newer type of soft lens material combining silicone (for high O2 permeability) and hydrogel (for comfort and water content). "Upgraded recipe" allowing much more oxygen through the silicone part, not just the water.

Step 6: Contact Lens Materials

The material a lens is made from dictates many of its properties and how it interacts with the eye.

Evolution of Materials:

  1. PMMA (Polymethyl Methacrylate): "Hard" lenses. Optically excellent, durable. Virtually NO oxygen permeability (Dk ≈ 0). Caused significant corneal edema. Rarely used now.
  2. RGP (Rigid Gas Permeable):
    • SA (Silicone Acrylate): Added silicone to PMMA to allow some O2 passage. More wettable but could flex/warp.
    • FSA (Fluoro-Silicone Acrylate): Added fluorine for better deposit resistance, stability, and higher Dk. Most common RGP type now.
    RGPs provide crisp vision and correct corneal astigmatism with their tear lens.
  3. Soft Hydrogel (Conventional Hydrogels):
    • Mainly HEMA-based. Flexible, comfortable.
    • Water Content is key: Oxygen passes through the water. Higher water content = higher Dk (up to a point).
    • FDA Groups 1-4: Classified by water content (low/high) and ionicity (ionic/non-ionic).
      • Ionic materials: Negatively charged surface, attract more protein deposits.
      • Non-ionic materials: Neutral surface, resist protein deposits better.
  4. Silicone Hydrogel (SiHy):
    • Revolutionized soft lenses by significantly increasing Dk.
    • Oxygen passes through the silicone component, not just water. Allows for much lower water content while maintaining high Dk.
    • Early SiHys had issues with stiffness (modulus) and wettability, but newer generations are much improved.

Other Important Material Properties:

  • Modulus: Stiffness or flexibility of the material. Higher modulus = stiffer lens. Affects comfort and handling. SiHys generally have higher modulus than HEMA.
  • Wettability: How well the lens surface maintains a smooth, even tear film. Poor wettability = dryness, deposits, blurry vision.
  • Surface Treatments/Technologies: Many lenses have special coatings or embedded agents to improve wettability and comfort.
Analogy - Materials & Clothing:
  • PMMA: A stiff plastic raincoat (no breathability).
  • RGP: A well-made leather jacket (some breathability, holds shape).
  • Hydrogel: A cotton t-shirt (comfortable, breathes through moisture).
  • SiHy: Modern athletic wear (very breathable synthetic, wicks moisture).

Quick Check: A patient is prone to protein deposits. Would you generally prefer an ionic or non-ionic hydrogel material for them, and why?

Answer

Generally, a non-ionic material would be preferred. Ionic materials have a negatively charged surface that tends to attract positively charged proteins from the tear film, leading to more deposits.

Step 7: Optics of Contact Lenses

How do contact lenses actually correct vision?

  • Basic Principle: Like spectacles, CLs add or subtract focusing power to bring light to a sharp focus on the retina.
  • Vertex Distance: The distance from the back surface of a spectacle lens to the front surface of the cornea.
    • For CLs, vertex distance is essentially zero (lens is on the cornea).
    • CRITICAL for powers +/- 4.00 D or higher. A spectacle Rx needs to be compensated (adjusted) for the change in vertex distance when prescribing CLs.
      • High Minus spectacle Rx -> CL Rx will be LESS minus.
      • High Plus spectacle Rx -> CL Rx will be MORE plus.
  • Effective Power: The focusing power of a lens at a specific plane (e.g., corneal plane for CLs vs. spectacle plane).
  • Lacrimal Lens (Tear Lens):
    • This is VERY important for RGP lenses.
    • The space between the back surface of an RGP lens and the front surface of the cornea fills with tears. This "tear lens" has its own refractive power.
    • An RGP lens can often correct significant amounts of corneal astigmatism because the tear lens neutralizes the corneal irregularity, creating a smooth spherical front refracting surface.
    • Soft lenses generally drape over the cornea, so they don't create a significant tear lens to correct astigmatism (unless they are toric designs).
  • Spherical Aberration: An optical imperfection where light rays passing through the periphery of a lens focus at a different point than rays passing through the center. Aspheric CL designs can help reduce this.
Analogy - Vertex Distance: Imagine holding a magnifying glass. The closer you hold it to the paper (reducing vertex distance), the "stronger" its effect seems. Same idea for spectacle power vs. CL power at the cornea.

Step 8: Indications for Contact Lens Wear

Why would someone wear contact lenses? Many reasons!

Category Specific Examples Brief Explanation
Optical / Refractive Myopia (nearsightedness), Hyperopia (farsightedness), Astigmatism Primary reason: To see clearly without glasses.
Anisometropia Significant difference in Rx between the two eyes. CLs reduce image size differences (aniseikonia) better than glasses.
Irregular Astigmatism Cornea is not smoothly curved (e.g., Keratoconus, post-surgery). RGPs or specialty CLs can provide much better vision than glasses.
Aphakia Absence of the eye's natural lens (e.g., after cataract surgery without an IOL). CLs can provide the needed high plus power.
Therapeutic / Bandage Corneal abrasions, recurrent erosions, bullous keratopathy, post-surgical healing Specially designed (often high Dk/t soft) lenses to protect the cornea, relieve pain, and promote healing.
Cosmetic / Prosthetic Changing eye color, masking corneal scars or iris defects (aniridia) Opaque tinted lenses for aesthetic purposes or to improve appearance of a disfigured eye. Still requires proper fitting!
Occupational Athletes, actors, certain professions where glasses are inconvenient or unsafe Freedom from glasses, wider field of view.
Sports Most sports Better peripheral vision, less risk of injury from broken glasses, don't fog up or get wet as easily.
Myopia Control Children with progressing myopia Specialized CL designs (multifocal, orthokeratology) to slow down the progression of nearsightedness. A growing indication!
