Step 9: Contraindications for Contact Lens Wear

These are reasons why a patient should NOT wear contact lenses, either temporarily or permanently.

Categories:

  • Absolute Contraindications: Conditions where CL wear is generally never advisable due to high risk of serious complications.
  • Relative Contraindications: Conditions where CL wear may be possible with careful management, special lens types, or once the condition resolves. Risk vs. benefit must be carefully weighed.
Condition/Factor Type (Absolute/Relative) Reasoning / CL Relevance
Active ocular infection/inflammation (e.g., keratitis, uveitis, severe conjunctivitis) Absolute High risk of worsening infection, corneal damage.
Severe Dry Eye Disease (DED) unresponsive to treatment Absolute to Relative (depends on severity & lens type) Extreme discomfort, corneal damage, lens intolerance. Specialty lenses might be an option in some managed cases.
Poor hygiene / Non-compliance (demonstrated) Absolute (if unchangeable) to Relative (if can be educated) High risk of infection and inflammatory complications. A major cause of CL problems!
Allergies to lens materials or care solutions Absolute (for that specific material/solution) Causes significant irritation, inflammation. May need to switch lens type or care system.
Inability to handle lenses (physical or cognitive) Absolute to Relative (depends on support available) Risk of injury, lens loss, improper care.
Certain Systemic Diseases (uncontrolled) Relative (often) E.g., Uncontrolled Diabetes (poor healing, corneal changes), Autoimmune diseases (Sjogren's - severe DED, Rheumatoid Arthritis). Requires careful co-management.
Pregnancy / Hormonal Changes Relative Can cause changes in corneal curvature, tear film, dry eye. Refit may be needed post-partum.
Adverse Environmental Conditions (dusty, chemical fumes) Relative Increased lens deposits, irritation, risk. May require specific lens types or limit wear.
Chronic Blepharitis / Meibomian Gland Dysfunction (MGD) Relative Poor tear film quality, deposits, discomfort. Needs to be managed before/during CL wear.
Corneal Hypoesthesia (reduced corneal sensitivity) Relative to Absolute Patient may not feel early signs of problems (e.g., abrasion, ulcer), leading to delayed treatment.
Study Tip - Case Scenarios: Think of a patient presenting with one of these conditions. What questions would you ask? What would your advice be regarding CLs?

Step 10: Rigid Gas Permeable (RGP) Contact Lens Design Principles

RGP lenses are custom-designed for each eye based on corneal shape and refractive error.

Analogy - RGP Design: Imagine a tiny, precisely shaped "custom bowl" that needs to sit perfectly centered and move just right on the "hill" of the cornea, allowing a thin layer of "water" (tears) underneath for comfort and optical correction.

Key RGP Design Parameters:

  • Back Optic Zone Radius (BOZR) / Base Curve (BC): The primary curve on the back surface. Chosen based on corneal curvature (K-readings). Determines the basic "fit" (alignment, steep, flat).
  • Back Optic Zone Diameter (BOZD) / Optic Zone Diameter (OZD): The size of the central BOZR area. Must cover the pupil.
  • Peripheral Curve System:
    • RGPs have multiple, progressively flatter curves towards the edge (e.g., 1st PC, 2nd PC, 3rd PC).
    • Each has a Peripheral Curve Radius (PCR) and Peripheral Curve Width (PCW).
    • Purpose: To allow the lens to align with the flattening peripheral cornea, promote tear exchange, and provide adequate edge lift/clearance so the edge doesn't dig into the cornea.
  • Overall Diameter (OAD): Total lens size. Influences centration and lid interaction.
  • Center Thickness (CT): Influenced by lens power (minus lenses thinner centrally, plus lenses thicker) and material. Affects Dk/t and lens flexure.
  • Edge Design: Shape and thickness of the very edge. Critical for comfort and lid interaction.
  • Spherical vs. Aspheric Designs:
    • Spherical: BOZR is a single radius of curvature.
    • Aspheric: BOZR gradually flattens from center to periphery (eccentricity). Can improve fit on some corneas, potentially reduce spherical aberration.
  • Lenticulation (for high power lenses): Modifying the anterior peripheral lens surface to reduce thickness and mass, improving comfort and centration (e.g., "minus carrier" for high plus lenses).
RGP Fitting Goal: To achieve good centration, adequate movement (0.5-1.5mm on blink), good tear exchange (seen with fluorescein), patient comfort, and clear vision.

Step 11: Soft Contact Lens Design Principles

Soft lenses are generally more "off-the-shelf" but design still matters for fit and performance.

Analogy - Soft Lens Design: Like choosing the right size and shape of a "flexible t-shirt" for your body. It needs to drape well without being too tight or too loose.

Key Soft Lens Design Considerations:

  • Base Curve (BC) & Diameter (Dia):
    • Fewer BC/Dia options available compared to RGPs.
    • The combination of BC and Dia determines the sagittal depth (sag) of the lens – how much it "vaults." This is a key factor in soft lens fitting.
    • A lens with a steeper BC and/or larger diameter will generally have a greater sagittal depth and fit tighter.
  • Material Drape: How the lens material conforms to the shape of the cornea. Stiffer materials (higher modulus) drape less.
  • Center Thickness (CT): Varies with power. Affects Dk/t and handling.
  • Edge Design: Tapered or rounded edges for comfort and to minimize lid interaction.
  • Optic Zone Diameter (OZD): Needs to be large enough to cover the pupil in various lighting.
  • Aspheric Optics: Many modern soft lenses use aspheric front surfaces to reduce spherical aberration and potentially improve vision quality.
Soft Lens Fitting Goals: Good centration, adequate movement (0.2-0.5mm on blink is typical, more subtle than RGP), full corneal coverage, patient comfort, and clear, stable vision. Over-refraction is key.

Step 12: Keratometry

Keratometry is a measurement of the curvature of the anterior central cornea (approx. central 3mm) in its two principal meridians.

Analogy - Keratometer: Like a specialized ruler that measures how "curved" or "flat" the very front of your eye's windshield is, and whether it's more curved in one direction than another (like a football vs. a basketball).

What it Tells Us ("K-readings"):

  • Corneal Curvature:
    • Expressed in millimeters (mm) of radius (e.g., 7.80 mm) - smaller mm = steeper curve.
    • OR in Diopters (D) (e.g., 43.25 D) - higher Diopters = steeper curve. (Uses a refractive index of ~1.3375 to convert).
  • Corneal Astigmatism: The difference in curvature between the two principal meridians.
    • Example: 43.00 D @ 180 / 45.00 D @ 090. This cornea has 2.00 D of astigmatism.
  • Orientation of Principal Meridians: The axes of the flattest and steepest curves (e.g., @180, @090).
  • Quality of Mires: The reflected images from the keratometer.
    • Clear and regular mires: Indicate a smooth, regular corneal surface.
    • Distorted, irregular, or "scissoring" mires: Suggest an irregular corneal surface (e.g., keratoconus, dry eye, corneal warpage). This is a very important qualitative finding!

Types of Corneal Astigmatism (based on K's):

  • With-the-Rule (WTR): Vertical meridian is steeper (e.g., 42.00 @ 180 / 44.00 @ 090). Common in younger people.
  • Against-the-Rule (ATR): Horizontal meridian is steeper (e.g., 44.00 @ 180 / 42.00 @ 090). More common with age.
  • Oblique Astigmatism: Principal meridians are not close to 90/180 (e.g., 43.00 @ 045 / 45.00 @ 135).
Astigmatism Rule: "The steeper meridian is closer to the axis of the MINUS cylinder in glasses." (This helps link K's to spectacle Rx).
Study Tip - Practice K's: If you have example K-readings, practice identifying the amount and type of astigmatism. Try to visualize what the mires might look like for regular vs. irregular astigmatism.

Keratometry Challenge: K-readings are: 7.50mm @ 010 / 7.20mm @ 100.
1. Which meridian is steeper?
2. What is the approximate dioptric difference (corneal astigmatism)? (Assume an average K of ~44D for conversion if needed for rough estimate, or use a converter if you have one).
3. What type of astigmatism is this likely to be considering the axes?

Answer & Rationale

1. The meridian with the smaller mm radius is steeper. So, 7.20mm @ 100 is steeper.
2. To estimate dioptric difference:

  • Power (D) = 337.5 / radius (mm)
  • Flat meridian (7.50mm): 337.5 / 7.50 = 45.00 D
  • Steep meridian (7.20mm): 337.5 / 7.20 = 46.875 D (approx 46.88 D)
  • Corneal Astigmatism: 46.88 D - 45.00 D = 1.88 D.

3. The principal meridians (010 and 100) are roughly 90 degrees apart but are not close to horizontal/vertical. This is Oblique Astigmatism. The steeper meridian is at 100 degrees.
