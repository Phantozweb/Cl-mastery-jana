Step 1: Corneal Anatomy

The cornea is the eye's transparent front window. It's avascular (no blood vessels) which helps its clarity. It handles most of the eye's focusing power.

Analogy: Think of the cornea as the strong, clear windshield of a car. It needs to be perfectly clear to see through and tough enough to protect what's inside.

Corneal Layers (Approx. Central Thickness: 0.52mm or 520 microns)

Mnemonic: Always Bring Cornflakes, Donuts & Eggs
(Actually, it's Epithelium, Bowman's, Stroma, Descemet's, Endothelium - so Eat Big Steaks, Drink Everything!)
Layer Approx. Thickness Key Features Regeneration? CL Relevance
1. Epithelium ~50 microns (5-7 cells) Outer barrier, smooth surface. Tight junctions. Yes, rapidly (days). First contact, SPK, abrasions.
2. Bowman's Layer ~8-14 microns Tough, acellular, collagen. No (scars). Permanent damage if breached.
3. Stroma ~450 microns (~90%) Collagen lamellae (regularly arranged for transparency), keratocytes, proteoglycans. Mainly water. Slowly (can scar). Site of edema, infiltrates, scars.
4. Descemet's Membrane ~3-12 microns (thickens with age) Endothelium's basement membrane. Elastic. Yes (by endothelium). Folds indicate edema/trauma.
5. Endothelium ~5 microns (single layer) Hexagonal cells. Na+/K+ pump maintains deturgescence. No significant regeneration (cells spread/enlarge). CRITICAL. Hypoxia damages pump -> edema. Polymegethism.

Innervation: Richly supplied by Trigeminal nerve (CN V1) - very sensitive!

Study Tip: Draw the corneal layers. Label each and write one key function and one CL implication next to it.

Step 2: Corneal Physiology

Physiology is how the cornea *works* to stay healthy and clear.

  • Transparency: Maintained by avascularity, regular stromal collagen, and deturgescence (relative dehydration ~78% water).
  • Oxygen (O2) Supply:
    • Open Eye: Atmosphere -> Tear Film -> Cornea
    • Closed Eye: Palpebral conjunctival vessels & Aqueous Humor (much lower O2).
  • Metabolism:
    • Aerobic (with O2): Preferred. Efficient energy. Epithelium & Endothelium are big O2 users.
    • Anaerobic (without enough O2): Inefficient. Produces lactic acid -> draws water -> edema.
  • Hydration Control (Deturgescence):
    Analogy - Endothelial Pump: Like a tiny sump pump in a basement, constantly pumping out excess water to keep the "stromal basement" from flooding (becoming edematous).
    • Epithelial barrier (keeps tears out).
    • Endothelial pump (pumps water into aqueous).
  • Tear Film: Provides O2, nutrients, lubrication, removes waste, antimicrobial.

Quick Check: What happens to corneal metabolism if oxygen supply is severely limited?

Answer

The cornea shifts to anaerobic metabolism, producing lactic acid. This buildup increases stromal osmolarity, drawing fluid into the stroma and causing corneal edema.

Step 3: Corneal Physiology AND Contact Lens Interactions

How does wearing a CL mess with normal corneal physiology?

Interaction Cause related to CL Key Physiological Effect(s)
Hypoxia (low O2) Lens barrier (low Dk/t = low O2 transmission) Edema, neovascularization, endothelial changes, anaerobic metabolism.
CO2 Buildup Lens traps CO2 Lowers pH, potential cell stress.
Altered Tear Exchange Lens presence, tight fit Waste buildup, reduced nutrients/O2, debris under lens.
Mechanical Effects Lens rubbing, poor edge, deposits Abrasions, SEALs, GPC.
Lens Deposits (Protein, Lipid) Tear film components sticking to lens Reduced Dk/t, discomfort, GPC, vision issues.
Analogy - CL as a Blanket: A thin, breathable blanket (high Dk/t lens) is fine. A thick, non-breathable plastic sheet (low Dk/t lens) will make you sweaty and unable to breathe well (hypoxia and edema).

Step 4: Introduction to Slit Lamp Biomicroscopy

The Slit Lamp is your #1 tool for examining the anterior eye. It's a stereo microscope with a controllable bright light source.

Analogy - Slit Lamp: Think of it as a detective's advanced magnifying glass with a built-in flashlight that can change shape and color. You're looking for "clues" about eye health.

Key Parts:

  • Observation System (binoculars, magnification)
  • Illumination System (light, slit controls, filters)
  • Mechanical System (joystick, rests)

Basic Illumination Techniques:

Technique Light Setup Primary Use
Diffuse Wide beam, diffuser General overview of eye & lids.
Direct Focal (Parallelepiped) Moderately wide slit (1-2mm) 3D view of corneal layers, lens. Look for opacities, infiltrates.
Direct Focal (Optic Section) Very thin slit (0.1-0.2mm) Optical "slice" to see depth of lesions, corneal thickness.
Indirect Illumination Focus scope next to light beam View subtle changes (e.g., microcysts) with scattered light.
Retroillumination Light reflects off deeper structure (iris/fundus) Highlights corneal opacities, vessels, CL deposits from behind.
Specular Reflection Angle of illumination = angle of observation View corneal endothelium (see cell shapes).

Key Filter: Cobalt Blue Filter - Used with sodium fluorescein (NaFl) dye to see corneal abrasions (they glow green!), assess RGP lens fit (tear pooling), and tear break-up time (TBUT).

Study Tip - Slit Lamp Visualization: For each illumination technique, try to imagine what you are trying to see and why that specific light setup helps. If possible, watch videos of slit lamp exams.

Challenge Question: You suspect a patient has subtle corneal microcysts due to chronic mild hypoxia. Which slit lamp illumination technique would be most helpful to visualize them?

Answer & Rationale

Indirect retroillumination or high magnification direct illumination with a parallelepiped and careful focusing. Microcysts are small, refractile bodies within the epithelium. Indirect retroillumination uses light scattered from the iris or fundus to highlight these subtle, transparent structures against the darker pupil or by showing them as "reversed" illumination. Careful direct illumination might also show them if they cause slight surface irregularity or have different refractive properties.
