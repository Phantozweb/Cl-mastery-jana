Step 17: Fitting of Spherical Soft Contact Lenses

Assessing how a soft lens sits and moves on the eye.

Trial Lens Selection:

  • Based on Keratometry (K's):
    • Flatter K's (e.g., < 42.00 D): May start with a flatter Base Curve (BC) option (e.g., 8.8 - 9.0 mm).
    • Average K's (e.g., 42.00 - 45.00 D): May start with a median BC (e.g., 8.4 - 8.7 mm).
    • Steeper K's (e.g., > 45.00 D): May start with a steeper BC option (e.g., 8.3 - 8.5 mm).
    • Always consult manufacturer's fitting guides as BC ranges vary.
  • Based on Horizontal Visible Iris Diameter (HVID): Lens diameter should typically be HVID + 2mm to ensure adequate corneal coverage.
  • Refractive Error: Select initial power based on compensated spectacle Rx.

Assessing the Fit (after ~10-20 mins settling): "The 3 C's & M"

Factor Ideal Fit Signs of a TIGHT Fit (Steep) Signs of a LOOSE Fit (Flat)
Centration (C) Lens centered over cornea, pupil well covered. Well-centered but may be immobile. Decentered (often inferiorly or temporally), may decenter significantly on blink.
Coverage (C) Cornea fully covered in all gaze positions. Lens extends 0.5-1mm onto sclera all around. Full coverage usually. Edge lift, limbal exposure, especially on upgaze or lateral gaze.
Movement (M)
  • Push-up Test: Lens moves freely when nudged with lower lid. Should move ~0.2-0.5 mm.
  • Blink Lag: Slight movement (~0.2-0.5 mm) downwards with each blink, then recenters quickly.
 Minimal or no movement on push-up. Little to no blink lag. Conjunctival drag/vessel blanching at limbus. Excessive movement (>1mm) on push-up. Excessive blink lag, lens may ride high or drop low. Unstable vision.
Patient Comfort Good comfort. Initial comfort may be okay, but can lead to redness, tightness sensation, blurry vision after removal (spectacle blur from edema). Awareness of lens edge, discomfort, fluctuating vision.
Vision Clear and stable. May be initially clear but can fluctuate or blur if edema occurs. Often unstable, blurry after blinks due to decentration.

Over-Refraction (OR): After confirming an acceptable fit, perform a spherical (and potentially cylindrical if vision isn't crisp) over-refraction to determine the final lens power.

Analogy - Soft Lens Fit:
  • Ideal Fit: A perfectly tailored, comfortable glove. Stays in place but allows some movement.
  • Tight Fit: A glove that's too small. Constricting, hard to move fingers.
  • Loose Fit: A glove that's too big. Sloppy, slides around.

Step 18: Effects of Parameter Changes in Spherical Soft Contact Lenses

Understanding how changing BC or Diameter affects the fit.

  • Sagittal Depth (Sag): The perpendicular distance between the geometric center of the back surface of the lens and the plane containing the lens edge. This is the primary determinant of soft lens fit.
    • Increased Sag = Tighter Fit
    • Decreased Sag = Looser Fit
Parameter Change Effect on Sagittal Depth Effect on Fit When to Make this Change
Steepen Base Curve (BC) (e.g., 8.6mm to 8.3mm) Increases Sag Tighter Fit If current lens is too loose.
Flatten Base Curve (BC) (e.g., 8.3mm to 8.6mm) Decreases Sag Looser Fit If current lens is too tight.
Increase Diameter (Dia) (keeping BC same) Increases Sag Tighter Fit (also improves coverage) If lens is loose and/or has poor coverage.
Decrease Diameter (Dia) (keeping BC same) Decreases Sag Looser Fit If lens is tight and edge is impinging. (Less common change).
Study Tip - Scenarios: If a patient's soft lens shows excessive movement and decenters inferiorly, what parameter(s) would you consider changing first? (Answer: Steepen BC or Increase Diameter).

Step 19: Fitting Spherical RGP Contact Lenses (Low Dk materials)

RGP fitting relies heavily on fluorescein pattern assessment.

Fluorescein Sodium:

  • A yellow-orange dye that glows bright green under cobalt blue light (from slit lamp or Burton lamp).
  • Instilled into the tear film. It highlights the thickness of the tear layer between the RGP lens and the cornea.
    • Bright Green: Thick tear layer = Clearance (lens is further from cornea).
    • Dark/Black Area: Thin or no tear layer = Touch/Bearing (lens is touching or very close to cornea).
    • Even, Thin Green: Alignment (lens closely follows corneal curvature).

Common RGP Fitting Philosophies & Ideal Patterns (Low Dk):

Fitting Philosophy Ideal Fluorescein Pattern (Static & Dynamic) Movement Rationale
Alignment Fit (Lid-Attached or Interpalpebral)
  • Apical (Central): Thin, even green alignment or minimal (<0.50D) clearance.
  • Mid-Peripheral: Good alignment.
  • Peripheral: Adequate band of edge clearance/lift (0.5-1.0mm wide) for tear exchange.
 1-1.5mm smooth movement with blink. Good centration. Distributes lens weight evenly, promotes good tear exchange, generally comfortable.
Slightly Steep Fit (Apical Clearance)
  • Apical: Definite central pool of fluorescein (clearance), like a "bull's eye."
  • Mid-Peripheral: May show some bearing/touch.
  • Peripheral: Adequate edge clearance.
 May be slightly less movement, good centration. Sometimes preferred for certain corneal shapes or to vault over a slightly irregular apex. Can reduce risk of central abrasion if lens decenters.
Slightly Flat Fit (Apical Touch/Bearing)
  • Apical: Central touch/bearing (dark area).
  • Mid-Peripheral: Excessive clearance ("moat").
  • Peripheral: May have excessive edge lift.
 Often excessive movement, poor centration (rides high or low). Generally undesirable. Can cause corneal abrasion, discomfort, spectacle blur. May be a temporary step in a very flat cornea fitting.

Dynamic Assessment: Observe pattern and movement immediately after a blink and as lens settles.

Step 20: Fitting Spherical RGP Contact Lenses (High Dk materials)

High Dk RGP materials offer better oxygen transmission but can have some different fitting considerations.

  • Increased Oxygen Benefits: Reduced risk of corneal hypoxia, better for extended wear (if applicable), good for compromised corneas.
  • Potential Challenges with some High Dk Materials:
    • Material Flexure: Some higher Dk materials are less rigid and may bend or "flex" on an astigmatic cornea, inducing unwanted residual astigmatism. This can make the over-refraction tricky.
    • Surface Wettability: Some silicone-containing materials can be inherently less wettable. May require plasma surface treatments or specific care solutions to maintain good wettability.
    • Deposit Resistance: Can vary. Fluorine in FSA materials helps.
    • Durability/Brittleness: Some high Dk materials might be slightly more prone to chipping or breaking than lower Dk ones.
  • Fitting Approach:
    • Fluorescein pattern assessment remains the same.
    • Be mindful of flexure during over-refraction, especially on toric corneas. Perform keratometry over the lens (on eye) to check for flexure if residual astigmatism is found.
    • Choose appropriate care systems recommended for the specific high Dk material.
Study Tip - Draw Fluorescein Patterns: Sketch out what an ideal alignment fit, a steep fit, and a flat fit would look like with fluorescein. Label the areas of touch, alignment, and clearance.

RGP Fit Scenario: You put an RGP lens on a patient. With fluorescein, you see a large, dark central area with a bright green ring around it in the mid-periphery, and good edge lift.
1. What type of fit is this (flat, steep, or aligned)?
2. What problem(s) might this cause for the patient?

Answer & Rationale

1. This describes a Flat Fit (Apical Touch/Bearing). The dark central area indicates the lens is resting heavily on the corneal apex, and the bright green "moat" in the mid-periphery shows excessive clearance there.
2. Problem(s) this might cause:

  • Discomfort: Due to central bearing and potential for edge awareness if movement is excessive.
  • Corneal Abrasion/Staining: Constant rubbing on the corneal apex can damage the epithelium.
  • Spectacle Blur: Corneal molding/warpage from the pressure.
  • Poor/Unstable Vision: If the lens decenters significantly.
  • Lens Ejection: If movement is too great.
