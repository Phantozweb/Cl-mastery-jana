These complications arise primarily from physical interaction of the lens with the ocular surface, solution incompatibilities, or tear film issues.

Superior Epithelial Arcuate Lesions (SEALs) / Epithelial Splitting

  • Signs: Arc-shaped (curved) epithelial break or heaped-up epithelium, typically in superior cornea (10 to 2 o'clock), often bilateral. Stains with fluorescein. May be asymptomatic or cause mild foreign body sensation.
  • Causes: Mechanical chafing from stiff upper edge of thicker, higher modulus soft lenses (especially some Silicone Hydrogels) interacting with superior limbus/conjunctiva during blinking. Tight upper lid.
  • Management: Discontinue current lens. Refit with lower modulus material, thinner edge design, or different lens design/diameter. Ensure lens is not too tight superiorly.

Epithelial Abrasions / Traumatic Keratitis

  • Signs: Sharp pain, foreign body sensation, tearing, redness, light sensitivity. Localized epithelial defect that stains brightly with fluorescein.
  • Causes: Improper insertion/removal technique, trapped foreign body under lens, damaged lens edge, overwear leading to epithelial breakdown.
  • Management: Discontinue CL wear. Prophylactic antibiotic drops often prescribed. Consider bandage CL for large abrasions. Re-educate on handling. Inspect lens for damage before reinsertion.

Mucin Balls / Pre-corneal Debris

  • Signs: Small, round, translucent "globules" trapped in the post-lens tear film, often seen with Silicone Hydrogel lenses, especially in EW. Can indent the epithelium (leaving "divots" or dimple veil staining after lens removal) but usually don't stain themselves. Generally asymptomatic or mild FBS.
  • Causes: Tear film debris (mucin, lipids) rolling up under the lens, particularly with tighter fitting or less mobile SiHy lenses.
  • Management: Usually benign. If excessive or causing symptoms/staining, consider looser fit, different material, more frequent replacement, or enhanced cleaning.

Solution-Induced Corneal Staining (SICS) / Preservative Associated Keratopathy

  • Signs: Diffuse, fine punctate corneal staining (SPK) often in a specific pattern (e.g., "bullseye" or ring around cornea for some MPS preservatives like PHMB binding to lens then releasing). Often bilateral. May cause dryness, irritation, redness.
  • Causes: Sensitivity or toxic reaction to preservatives (e.g., PHMB, Polyquad) or other components in multipurpose solutions (MPS) or care systems. More common with SiHy lenses due to material interactions.
  • Management: Discontinue offending solution. Switch to a different MPS (with different preservative) or a hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) based system (preservative-free once neutralized). Daily disposables eliminate solution issues.

Tight Lens Syndrome (TLS) / Acute Red Eye (related to CLARE, but specific to tight fit)

  • Signs: Often occurs after several hours of wear or upon waking. Redness (circumlimbal flush), pain/discomfort, hazy vision, lens immobile on push-up. Fluorescein may show conjunctival indentation where lens edge sits. May see trapped debris under lens.
  • Causes: Soft lens that is too steep or too large for the cornea, leading to insufficient movement, tear exchange, and oxygen. Can be exacerbated by dehydration of lens.
  • Management: IMMEDIATE lens removal. Refit with a looser lens (flatter BC, smaller diameter). Ensure adequate Dk/t. TLS can rapidly progress to more serious inflammatory or hypoxic complications if not addressed.

Contact Lens-Induced Dry Eye (CLIDE)

  • Signs: Symptoms of dryness (grittiness, burning, tired eyes, fluctuating vision) that are worse with CL wear or towards end of day. Reduced TBUT over lens, increased lens deposits, conjunctival staining.
  • Causes: Multifactorial. CLs disrupt normal tear film structure and evaporation. Lens material properties (wettability, water content), solution effects, pre-existing borderline dry eye. Environmental factors.
  • Management: Optimize lens material (e.g., lower water content SiHy that resists dehydration, or high water content hydrogel with good surface treatment for some). Change solutions. Prescribe ocular lubricants (CL-compatible). Manage underlying MGD/blepharitis. Reduce wear time. Consider daily disposables.
Study Tip - Mechanical vs. Inflammatory: For each complication, try to identify the primary trigger. Is it the physical lens rubbing (mechanical)? Or the body's reaction to something (inflammatory/allergic)? This helps in understanding management.

Step 42: Use of Specular Microscopy in Contact Lens Practice

Specular Microscopy is a non-invasive imaging technique used to visualize and analyze the corneal endothelium (the innermost single layer of cells).

Why Assess the Endothelium?

  • The endothelium is crucial for maintaining corneal transparency and hydration (via its pump function - Day 1).
  • Endothelial cells do not regenerate significantly in humans. Cell loss is permanent.
  • Chronic stress (e.g., from hypoxia, inflammation, trauma) can lead to morphological changes and cell loss.

Parameters Analyzed:

  • Endothelial Cell Density (ECD): Number of cells per square millimeter (cells/mmÂ²).
    • Normal adult: ~2000-3000 cells/mmÂ². Decreases with age.
    • Concern if ECD drops significantly or below critical levels (~500-800 cells/mmÂ² for maintaining clarity).
  • Polymegathism: Variation in cell size. Increased variation indicates stress. Often measured by Coefficient of Variation (CV) of cell area (higher CV = more polymegathism). Normal CV < ~0.30.
  • Pleomorphism: Variation in cell shape from the ideal hexagonal. Percentage of hexagonal cells is assessed. Normal >50-60% hexagonal. Stressed cells become less hexagonal.
  • Other findings: Guttata (excrescences on Descemet's, seen in Fuchs' dystrophy), cell drop-out areas, inflammatory cells.

When is Specular Microscopy Useful in CL Practice?

  • Baseline assessment before fitting Extended Wear (EW) or Continuous Wear (CW) lenses due to higher hypoxic risk.
  • Monitoring long-term CL wearers, especially those with history of low Dk/t lens wear or complications.
  • Pre-operative assessment for intraocular surgery (e.g., cataract) if patient is a long-term CL wearer.
  • Investigating unexplained corneal edema.
  • Assessing patients with known endothelial dystrophies (e.g., Fuchs').
Analogy - Endothelium & Cobblestones: Think of the endothelium as a perfectly laid cobblestone street with tightly packed, uniform hexagonal stones. Stress (hypoxia, etc.) is like some stones getting damaged, others having to stretch and change shape to fill gaps, and some being lost altogether, making the "street" less stable and functional.

Step 43: Use of Pachymetry in Contact Lens Practice

Pachymetry is the measurement of corneal thickness (CCT - Central Corneal Thickness).

Methods of Measurement:

  • Ultrasound Pachymetry: Uses sound waves. Probe touches cornea. Considered very accurate.
  • Optical Pachymetry: Non-contact.
    • Slit lamp mounted optical pachymeters.
    • Scheimpflug imaging (e.g., Pentacam) - provides a full corneal thickness map.
    • Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT) - provides cross-sectional images and thickness maps.

Normal CCT Values:

  • Average CCT is around 520-560 microns (Âµm). Can vary significantly between individuals.

Why Measure Corneal Thickness in CL Practice?

  • Detecting Corneal Edema:
    • An increase in CCT from baseline indicates corneal swelling (edema).
    • Useful for monitoring patients at risk of hypoxia (e.g., EW/CW wearers, tight lens fits, low Dk/t lenses). A significant increase (e.g., >5-10%) is a concern.
  • Monitoring Keratoconus:
    • KC involves progressive corneal thinning, especially at the cone apex. Pachymetry helps track progression.
  • Pre/Post Refractive Surgery Assessment:
    • Baseline CCT is vital for determining suitability for procedures like LASIK (which involves creating a flap and ablating stroma).
    • Post-op measurements can assess actual stromal bed thickness.
  • Glaucoma Management: CCT influences intraocular pressure (IOP) measurements (thinner corneas can give falsely low IOP readings with Goldmann tonometry, thicker ones falsely high).
  • Baseline measurement before initiating certain CL wear modalities.
Study Tip - Instrument Application: For both Specular Microscopy and Pachymetry, create a list of 3 specific patient scenarios where you would find that instrument particularly valuable in your CL practice.

Clinical Scenario: A long-term PMMA lens wearer (fitted 20 years ago) presents for a refit into modern RGP materials. Their corneas look clear on slit lamp with no obvious edema, but you want to be thorough. Which of today's advanced instruments would be most indicated to assess potential long-term effects of their old lenses, and what specific findings would you be looking for?

Answer & Rationale

The most indicated instrument would be Specular Microscopy.
Rationale: PMMA lenses have virtually no oxygen permeability (Dk=0). Long-term wear of PMMA lenses is highly associated with chronic corneal hypoxia, which primarily stresses the corneal endothelium.
Specific Findings to Look For:

  • Reduced Endothelial Cell Density (ECD): Compared to age-matched norms.
  • Increased Polymegathism: Significant variation in cell size (high CV).
  • Increased Pleomorphism: Reduced percentage of hexagonal cells.
Pachymetry could also be useful to get a baseline CCT and rule out any subclinical edema, but specular microscopy directly assesses the endothelial layer which is most at risk from chronic PMMA wear.
