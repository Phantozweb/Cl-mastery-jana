Detailed Quick Recall Notes: Contact Lens Steps 45-50

Step 45: Contact Lens Modification of Finished Lenses (RGPs)

RGP Modification In-Office Adjustment Polishing (Surface, Edge) Power Adjustment Edge Shaping Blending PCs Spinner Unit
Skill & Caution: RGP modification requires specific tools, skill, and careful execution to avoid ruining the lens. Practice on old lenses is essential.

Purpose & Appropriateness:

  • Making minor adjustments to RGP lens parameters in-office to improve fit, comfort, or vision.
  • Can save time and cost compared to reordering from the lab for small changes.
  • Appropriate when a specific, identifiable issue can be addressed by modification (e.g., slight edge discomfort, minor power tweak, light surface deposits).
  • NOT for major parameter errors, significant power changes, or if lens is fundamentally incorrect.

Common In-Office RGP Modifications & Tools:

ModificationPurposeTools & CompoundsKey Considerations
Surface Polishing Remove light deposits/scratches, improve wettability. Spinner unit, soft sponge tools, fine polishing compounds (e.g., Silvo, Xpal). Can slightly alter power (add plus/flatten) if overdone. Use light pressure.
Edge Polishing / Shaping Smooth rough edge, reshape edge profile (round, taper) for comfort. Spinner unit, shaped tools (cone, flat, round), polishing compounds. Critical for comfort. Aim for smooth, well-blended edge.
Power Adjustment (Minor) Add small amounts of plus power (up to ~+0.50D to +0.75D) by flattening anterior surface. Add minus is difficult/lab only. Spinner unit, specific tools for anterior surface polishing, compounds. Requires precise technique. Re-verify power frequently.
Blending Peripheral Curves (PCs) Smooth transitions between PC zones for comfort or better tear flow. Spinner unit, cone/round tools, appropriate compounds. Careful not to alter PCRs or PCWs significantly unless intended.
Reducing Overall Diameter (OAD) - Very Minor Slightly decrease lens size if marginally too large. Grinding stone/tool on spinner, then re-polish edge. Difficult to do evenly. Only for very small changes. Usually better to reorder.
Adding Minus Power to Edge (Edge Lift Increase) Effectively flattens the very peripheral edge to increase lift. Specific edge tools, careful technique. Can improve tear exchange or alleviate tight peripheral fit.

General Modification Process:

  1. Identify specific problem & desired change.
  2. Select appropriate tools and compounds.
  3. Perform modification carefully on spinner unit.
  4. Clean lens thoroughly.
  5. Re-verify ALL relevant parameters (BC, Power, CT, Edge, Surface) after modification.
  6. Document changes made.
Analogy - RGP Modification like Fine-Tuning a Custom-Made Wooden Toy: "Imagine you have a beautiful custom wooden car (RGP lens).
  • Polishing: Buffing out a tiny scratch on the paint or making the wheels (edges) smoother so it rolls better (comfort).
  • Power Adjustment: Slightly sanding the front to change its shape just a tiny bit (minor power change).
  • You wouldn't try to build a whole new car in your workshop (major changes go to the lab), just make small improvements."
Takeaway: In-office RGP modification can be a valuable skill for fine-tuning lens fit and comfort, but it requires proper training, equipment, and a cautious approach to avoid damaging the lens or significantly altering its intended design. Always verify post-modification.
Self-Check: A patient complains their new RGP lens feels "sharp" on one eyelid. What in-office modification might you consider, and what would you check before and after?

You might consider an Edge Polish / Shaping.
Before: Carefully inspect the lens edge under magnification (slit lamp or stereomicroscope) to identify any roughness, nicks, or an overly sharp/poorly shaped profile. Also, verify all other parameters are correct.
After: Re-inspect the edge to ensure it's smooth and well-contoured. Re-verify that other parameters like power or BOZR haven't been inadvertently altered. Assess patient comfort with the modified lens on-eye.

Step 46: Instrumentation in Contact Lens Practice (Overview)

Slit Lamp Keratometer Topographer Radiuscope Lensometer Pachymeter Specular Microscope Trial Lens Sets Diagnostic Tools

Core Instruments & Their Primary Functions:

(This is a recap – refer to earlier steps for detailed descriptions)

  • Slit Lamp Biomicroscope: Workhorse! Anterior eye health assessment, lens fit evaluation (soft & RGP with fluorescein), complication detection.
  • Keratometer: Measures central corneal curvature (K-readings). Guides initial BC selection.
  • Corneal Topographer: Detailed corneal surface map. For irregular corneas (KC), post-surgical, advanced RGP/scleral design.
  • Radiuscope: Verifies RGP Base Curve (BOZR).
  • Lensometer (Focimeter): Measures spectacle and CL power.
  • Pachymeter: Measures corneal thickness (CCT). Detects edema, monitors KC/post-op.
  • Specular Microscope: Assesses corneal endothelium (cell density, morphology).
  • Trial Lens Sets:
    • Soft Lens Trial Sets: Range of powers, BCs, Dias for common disposable/FR lenses.
    • RGP Trial Sets: Systematically arranged diagnostic lenses of known parameters for fitting.
  • Ancillary Tools:
    • Fluorescein Sodium Strips: For RGP fitting, corneal staining, TBUT.
    • Burton Lamp / Handheld Cobalt Blue Light & Magnifier: Gross fluorescein assessment.
    • HVID Ruler: Measures Horizontal Visible Iris Diameter for soft lens diameter selection.
    • PD Ruler: Measures interpupillary distance.
    • Measuring Magnifiers/Reticles: For RGP parameter verification (OAD, OZD, PCW).
    • Thickness Gauges: For RGP CT verification.
Analogy - ECP's Instrument Array like a Mechanic's Garage: "Each instrument is a specialized tool:
  • Slit Lamp: The main diagnostic computer and high-powered inspection light.
  • Keratometer/Topographer: Tools to measure the 'chassis alignment' (corneal shape).
  • Radiuscope/Lensometer: Tools to check if the 'new parts' (lenses) are made to spec.
  • Pachymeter/Specular Microscope: Tools to check the 'structural integrity' and 'engine health' of the eye itself.
  • Trial Sets: A box of 'sample parts' to see what fits best before ordering custom."
Takeaway: A comprehensive CL practice relies on a range of specialized instruments for accurate assessment of the eye and contact lenses, enabling precise fitting, effective troubleshooting, and proactive management of ocular health.
Self-Check: You suspect an RGP lens is fitting too flat based on excessive movement and a central touch pattern with fluorescein. Which instrument would you use to confirm the actual Base Curve of that RGP lens?

You would use a Radiuscope to verify the Back Optic Zone Radius (BOZR) or Base Curve of the RGP lens to see if it was manufactured correctly according to what was ordered, or if the ordered BC was indeed too flat for the cornea.

Step 47: Care of Contact Lenses – Deeper Dive into Contact Lens Solutions

Multipurpose Solutions (MPS) Hydrogen Peroxide (H2O2) Systems RGP Solutions Saline Disinfectants Cleaners (Surfactants) Wetting Agents Preservatives Compatibility

Multipurpose Solutions (MPS) for Soft Lenses:

Combine cleaning, rinsing, disinfection, and storing functions. Still require manual rubbing.

  • Key Components & Functions:
    • Disinfectants/Preservatives: Kill microbes. E.g., PHMB, Polyquaternium-1 (Polyquad™), Aldox™, Alexidine. (Can cause SICS/sensitivity).
    • Surfactants (Cleaners): Loosen debris/deposits. E.g., Poloxamine, Poloxamer.
    • Wetting Agents/Lubricants: Enhance surface wettability, comfort. E.g., HPMC, Hyaluronan, Tetronic.
    • Buffers: Maintain pH for comfort & disinfectant efficacy. E.g., borate, phosphate.
    • Chelating Agents: Bind/remove calcium. E.g., EDTA.
